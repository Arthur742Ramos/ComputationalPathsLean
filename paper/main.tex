\begin{abstract}
We survey the theory of \emph{computational paths}---a proof-relevant
account of equality in type theory in which every equality witness carries
an explicit trace of elementary rewrite steps.  Starting from the framework
of labelled natural deduction, where the judgement $t =_s u : A$ records
\emph{why} two proof-terms are equal, we develop a complete rewrite calculus
on the resulting path expressions.  The calculus comprises 77 named rewrite
rules, organised into eight categories covering the groupoid core, type
constructor interactions, transport, and congruence.  The system is proved
to be both terminating and confluent, yielding unique normal forms and
decidable path equivalence.

The higher algebraic structure of computational paths is then developed
in stages: paths between terms form a weak groupoid; rewrite derivations
between paths (2-cells) give rise to a weak 2-groupoid with vertical
and horizontal composition; 3-cells provide coherence data including the
pentagon and triangle identities; and the entire tower assembles into a
weak $\omega$-groupoid in the sense of Batanin--Leinster, with
stabilisation above dimension~3.  The Eckmann--Hilton argument establishes
commutativity in the double loop space.  We derive the J-eliminator
(path induction) from the contractibility of the based path space,
demonstrate the failure of the Uniqueness of Identity Proofs for the
$\Path$ type, and compute fundamental groups of combinatorial spaces
($\pi_1(S^1) \cong \Z$, $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \Z \times \Z$,
$\pi_1(\mathbb{R}P^2) \cong \Z/2\Z$).  The central results have been
formalised in Lean~4 (over 10{,}000 theorems, no axioms).

This paper announces the results of the monograph
\emph{Computational Paths: The Calculus of Equality}
(College Publications, 2026).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{The problem: equality and its reasons}

The Curry--Howard correspondence~\cite{Curry34,Howard80} establishes
a dictionary between logic and type theory: propositions correspond to
types, proofs to programs, and proof simplification to computation
($\beta$-reduction).  This dictionary has been enormously fruitful---it
underlies modern proof assistants such as Coq~\cite{Coq}, Agda~\cite{Agda},
and Lean~\cite{deMoura2021}---but it leaves a fundamental question
unanswered.  If proofs correspond to programs and proof simplification
corresponds to computation, then what corresponds to \emph{equality of
computations}?

Consider two computations of the same result.  Both begin with the same
expression and end at the same normal form, but the intermediate steps may
differ: one reduces the outermost redex first, the other an inner one.
The Church--Rosser theorem~\cite{ChurchRosser36} guarantees that the two
reduction sequences can be brought to a common result, but says nothing
about the sequences themselves.  Are these witnesses to equality---these
traces of computation---mathematically meaningful in their own right?

The theory of \emph{computational paths} answers this question
affirmatively.  The sequences of rewrite steps that transform one term
into another are reified as formal objects---computational paths---which
are themselves subject to a rich algebraic theory.  By extending the
Curry--Howard dictionary one level further, we obtain:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcp{4.2cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Logic (extended)} & & \textbf{Type Theory (extended)} \\
\midrule
Equality of proofs & $\leftrightarrow$ & Path between terms \\
Reason for equality & $\leftrightarrow$ & Computational path \\
Equality of reasons & $\leftrightarrow$ & Path between paths (2-cell) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
It is at the third row that the theory begins.  The insight that ``reasons
for equality'' can themselves be compared opens the door to an infinite
hierarchy of higher-dimensional structure---precisely the structure of a
weak $\omega$-groupoid~\cite{Grothendieck83,Batanin98,Leinster04}.

This hierarchy is intimately connected to a fundamental observation
of Voevodsky~\cite{Voevodsky2014HTF}: the notion of equality itself
must be stratified.  Equality is appropriate for
\emph{elements}---individuals---but fails for collections; isomorphism
is appropriate for collections but fails for collections of collections.
This leads to a theory of iterated $n$-equivalences which are the correct
replacements at each level.  The $\omega$-groupoid structure that emerges
from computational paths provides a concrete, syntactic realisation of
precisely this hierarchy: 1-cells capture equality of terms, 2-cells
capture equivalence of proofs, 3-cells capture coherence of equivalences,
and so on through all finite levels.

\subsection{Historical roots and motivation}

The roots of this work lie in de~Queiroz's introduction of the judgement
form $t =_s u : A$ in the late 1980s~\cite{deQueiroz88:dialectica,deQueiroz89,deQueiroz90:phd},
where the subscript~$s$ records the \emph{rewrite reason}---an explicit,
structured witness to the equality of proof-terms~$t$ and~$u$.  This
innovation took place within the broader programme of labelled natural
deduction (LND)~\cite{GabbaydeQueiroz92:c-h:full,Gabbay96:LDS}, which
enriches traditional natural deduction by carrying proof-terms alongside
formulas.  The key observation was that rewrite reasons are not mere
notational decorations but first-class mathematical entities: they can be
composed, inverted, and compared.

Building on this foundation, de~Queiroz and
de~Oliveira~\cite{deQueiroz94,deQueiroz2012,deOliveiraDeQueiroz99,deOliveiraDeQueiroz05}
developed the equational fragment of labelled deduction over several decades,
establishing normalisation procedures and proof transformations.
The identification of the identity type with the type of computational paths
was made explicit in~\cite{deQueirozDeOliveiraRamos2016,RamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2017},
and the full elaboration---including the 77-rule rewrite system,
confluence, the $\omega$-groupoid structure, and the Lean~4
formalisation---was carried out
in~\cite{Ramos2019thesis,deVerasRamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2023,RamosDeVerasDeQueirozDeOliveira2025omega,RamosDeOliveiraDeQueirozDeVeras2025lean}.

The dialogical reading of proof-term reduction, developed in~\cite{deQueiroz91:meaning,deQueiroz01:meaning,deQueiroz08:reduction},
connects this programme to Wittgenstein's ``meaning as use'' principle:
introduction rules play the role of a Defender's assertion, elimination
rules play the role of an Attacker's challenge, and the reductum gives
the continuation.  This makes explicit the ``meaning as use'' principle:
the meaning of each connective is fixed by the admissible challenges
and by how those challenges are answered.  The 77-rule system for
computational paths can be seen as a complete specification of the
``grammar of equality''---it tells us everything there is to know about
how equality proofs can be combined, decomposed, and compared.

\begin{remark}[Historical note]
The idea that proof-term reduction carries semantically significant
content goes back at least to Prawitz's \emph{Natural
Deduction}~\cite{Prawitz65}.  The further idea that the \emph{paths}
of reduction are worth studying as objects was advanced by
de~Queiroz~\cite{deQueiroz89,deQueiroz90:phd}, building on earlier insights
by Lambek and Scott~\cite{LambekScott86}, who wrote: ``In writing
$f : A \to B$ we think of~$f$ as the `reason' why~$A$ entails~$B$.''
The present work is the full elaboration of the programme implicit in
this remark.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Scope and organisation}

This paper is a survey and announcement of the results established in the
monograph \emph{Computational Paths: The Calculus of Equality}
(A.~F.~Ramos, R.~J.~G.~B.~de~Queiroz, A.~G.~de~Oliveira, and
D.~M.~Gabbay; College Publications, 2026).  It presents the main
definitions, theorems, and constructions in a self-contained form suitable
for a broad audience in mathematical logic, type theory, and related areas.

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:lnd} presents the labelled natural deduction framework.
Section~\ref{sec:paths} introduces computational paths and their
fundamental operations.
Section~\ref{sec:rewrite} describes the 77-rule rewrite system in full
detail, presenting the complete rules for each category.
Section~\ref{sec:confluence} establishes confluence and termination with
detailed proofs.
Section~\ref{sec:groupoid} develops the groupoid structure with full
definitions, proofs, and examples.
Section~\ref{sec:higher} constructs the higher cells, the weak
2-groupoid structure, whiskering and horizontal composition, and the

Section~\ref{sec:omega} proves the weak $\omega$-groupoid theorem with

Section~\ref{sec:j-uip} derives path induction, analyses transport and

Section~\ref{sec:applications} presents applications to fundamental group

Section~\ref{sec:formalization} reports on the Lean~4 formalisation with
architectural details.
Section~\ref{sec:perspectives} discusses open problems and future directions.
Appendix~\ref{app:rules} provides the complete list of all 77 rewrite rules
in tabular form.

\section{Labelled Natural Deduction}\label{sec:lnd}
% =========================================================================

The framework within which computational paths arise is that of
\emph{labelled natural deduction} (LND), developed by de~Queiroz
and Gabbay~\cite{GabbaydeQueiroz92:c-h:full} and subsequently extended
by de~Queiroz and de~Oliveira~\cite{deQueiroz94,deQueiroz2012} over
several decades.  LND enriches ordinary natural deduction with an extra
dimension: every formula in a deduction carries a \emph{label}---a term
from an associated functional calculus that records the structure of
the proof.

\subsection{Three judgement forms}

While the standard Curry--Howard correspondence employs two basic
judgement forms---$\Gamma \vdash t : A$ (``$t$ proves $A$'') and
$\Gamma \vdash t = u : A$ (``$t$ and $u$ are equal proofs'')---the LND
framework introduces a third, richer form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:labelled-equality}
\Gamma \vdash t =_s u : A
\end{equation}
where the subscript~$s$ is the \emph{rewrite reason}: an explicit,
structured witness to the equality of~$t$ and~$u$.

\begin{definition}[Labelled equality judgement]\label{def:labelled-eq}
A \emph{labelled equality judgement} is an expression of the form
$\Gamma \vdash t =_s u : A$, where $\Gamma$ is a context, $t$ and $u$
are proof-terms of type~$A$, and $s$ is a rewrite reason---a term in
the language of paths denoting a specific sequence of rewrite steps
transforming~$t$ into~$u$.
\end{definition}

The architecture of LND can be summarised as a two-dimensional system:
the \emph{horizontal} dimension consists of formulas and the logical
calculus, while the \emph{vertical} dimension consists of labels and the
functional calculus.  This parallels Frege's
\emph{Grundgesetze}~\cite{Frege93:GrundI} (terms) alongside
\emph{Begriffsschrift}~\cite{Frege79:Begr} (formulas)---a point
emphasised in~\cite{deQueiroz92:wittgenstein15}.

\subsection{Proof-terms and reduction rules}

The base system is the simply-typed $\lambda$-calculus with products
($A \times B$), coproducts ($A + B$), the empty type ($\mathbf{0}$), and
the unit type ($\mathbf{1}$).  The $\beta$-reduction rules capture the
computational content of proofs:
\begin{align}
(\lambda x.\, t)\, u &\to_\beta t[u/x] \tag{$\beta_\to$} \\
\pi_i(\langle t_1, t_2 \rangle) &\to_\beta t_i \tag{$\beta_{\times,i}$} \\
\mathsf{case}(\mathsf{inl}(t), x.u_1, y.u_2) &\to_\beta u_1[t/x]
  \tag{$\beta_{+,1}$} \\
\mathsf{case}(\mathsf{inr}(t), x.u_1, y.u_2) &\to_\beta u_2[t/y]
  \tag{$\beta_{+,2}$}
\end{align}
Each $\beta$-rule expresses the harmony between introduction and
elimination rules in the sense of Prawitz~\cite{Prawitz65}: when an
introduction is immediately followed by an elimination of the same
connective, the resulting ``detour'' can be simplified.

The fundamental metatheoretic properties of the base system are:

\begin{theorem}[Subject reduction]\label{thm:subject-reduction}
If\/ $\Gamma \vdash t : A$ and $t \to_\beta u$, then\/ $\Gamma \vdash u : A$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Church--Rosser for the base system]\label{thm:church-rosser-base}
The $\beta$-reduction relation is confluent: if\/ $t \to_\beta^* u_1$
and $t \to_\beta^* u_2$, then there exists~$v$ such that\/
$u_1 \to_\beta^* v$ and $u_2 \to_\beta^* v$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
The proof uses the parallel reduction technique of Tait and
Martin-L\"of~\cite{Tait67}: one defines a ``complete development'' $t^*$ that
simultaneously reduces all redexes, and shows that any single parallel
reduction step from~$t$ can be completed to~$t^*$.

\begin{remark}[Dialogical reading]
The $\beta$-reduction rules admit a dialogical
reading~\cite{deQueiroz91:meaning,deQueiroz01:meaning,deQueiroz08:reduction}:
introduction rules play the role of a Defender's assertion, elimination
rules play the role of an Attacker's challenge, and the reductum gives
the continuation.  This makes explicit the ``meaning as use'' principle:
the meaning of each connective is fixed by the admissible challenges
and by how those challenges are answered.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Abstraction discipline and substructural logics}

A significant observation emphasised in Gabbay's LDS
framework~\cite{Gabbay96:LDS} is that the $\lambda$-abstraction rule
admits variation.  By restricting which abstractions are permitted, one
obtains substructural logics:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Restriction on $\lambda$-abstraction} & \textbf{Resulting logic} \\
\midrule
Exactly one use (no vacuous, no contraction) & Linear logic~\cite{Girard87:tcs} \\
At least one use (no vacuous abstraction) & Relevant logic \\
At most one use (no contraction) & Affine logic \\
No restriction & Intuitionistic logic \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
This shows that the computational interpretation is not limited to
intuitionistic logic.  The common thread is that \emph{modus ponens}
(application) remains unchanged while the introduction rules differ in
their assumptions about resource use.  The use of abstractors to make
arbitrary names lose their identity goes back to Frege's
\emph{Grundgesetze}~\cite{Frege93:GrundI}: the binding transforms a
name into a mere place-marker for arguments in
substitutions~\cite{deQueiroz92:wittgenstein15}.

\subsection{From reduction sequences to computational paths}

The transition from proof theory to the theory of computational paths
is driven by a single observation: the reduction sequences of LND
carry natural algebraic structure.  Given sequences $p : t \leadsto u$
and $q : u \leadsto v$, one can compose them to form $p \cdot q : t
\leadsto v$; given $p : t \leadsto u$, one can reverse it to form
$p^{-1} : u \leadsto t$; and there is a trivial zero-step sequence
$\refl_t : t \leadsto t$.  These operations satisfy the laws of a
\emph{groupoid}---associativity, identity, and inversion---and the
congruence operation $f_*(p)$ satisfies functoriality.  Making this
structure precise is the task of the next section.

\begin{example}[Two distinct rewrite reasons]\label{ex:two-reasons}
Consider the term $t \equiv (\lambda x.\, (\lambda y.\, y\, x)\, z)\, w$.
There are two natural reduction paths to the normal form $z\, w$:

\textbf{Reason $s_1$} (outer-first):
\[
(\lambda x.\, (\lambda y.\, y\, x)\, z)\, w
\;\xrightarrow{\;\beta\;}
(\lambda y.\, y\, w)\, z
\;\xrightarrow{\;\beta\;}
z\, w
\]

\textbf{Reason $s_2$} (inner-first):
\[
(\lambda x.\, (\lambda y.\, y\, x)\, z)\, w
\;\xrightarrow{\;\beta\;}
(\lambda x.\, z\, x)\, w
\;\xrightarrow{\;\beta\;}
z\, w
\]

Both reasons witness the same equality $t = z\, w$, but they differ in
intermediate terms and reduction order.  The theory of computational paths
takes this difference seriously: $s_1$ and $s_2$ are genuinely different
mathematical objects.
\end{example}

\section{Computational Paths: Definition and Operations}\label{sec:paths}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Steps, paths, and path expressions}

The construction proceeds in two stages.  First, we identify the
\emph{atomic steps}---primitive rewrite operations.  Second, we define
\emph{path expressions}---compound terms built from atomic steps using
algebraic operations.

\begin{definition}[Elementary rewrite step]\label{def:step}
An \emph{elementary rewrite step} in a type~$A$ is a triple $(a, b, h)$
consisting of a source $a : A$, a target $b : A$, and a justification~$h$
recording one designated atomic equality step.  We write $\Step(A)$ for the
type of all steps in~$A$.
\end{definition}

The atomic steps for the simply-typed $\lambda$-calculus fall into two
families:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Computational steps}: $\beta$-reduction, $\eta$-expansion,
  and their counterparts for products and sums.
\item \emph{Structural steps}: congruence rules ($\xi$-steps,
  $\mu$-steps, $\nu$-steps) that propagate computation through compound
  terms.
\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}[Computational path]\label{def:path}
A \emph{computational path} from~$a$ to~$b$ in a type~$A$ is a pair
$\langle \sigma, \pi \rangle$ where $\sigma$ is a finite list of
elementary rewrite steps and $\pi : a = b$ is a propositional equality
witness.  We write $\Path(a, b)$ for the type of all paths from~$a$
to~$b$.
\end{definition}

This definition separates two concerns.  The \emph{proof} component
$\pi$ certifies the \emph{fact} that $a$ equals~$b$; it is
proof-irrelevant (living in $\Prop$).  The \emph{trace} component
$\sigma$ records \emph{how} the equality is witnessed; it is
proof-relevant and carries the computational content.

\begin{remark}[The two-level structure]\label{rem:two-level}
The framework maintains a deliberate two-level structure:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Computational paths} ($\Path$) are proof-relevant: distinct
  paths between the same endpoints are genuinely different objects.
\item \textbf{Propositional equality} ($\Eq$) is proof-irrelevant: any
  two proofs of $a = b$ are themselves equal (UIP holds for $\Eq$).
\end{enumerate}
The $\Path$ type does \emph{not} satisfy UIP---a fact proved in
\S\ref{ssec:uip-failure}.  The proof-relevant structure comes from the
trace component, not from any failure of UIP at the level of $\Eq$.
This design allows the phenomena of higher-dimensional type theory to
be studied within a setting compatible with classical foundations.

This two-level structure echoes Voevodsky's two-level type theory~\cite{Voevodsky2013,AnnenkovCapriottiKrausSattler2023},
which similarly separates a strict (proof-irrelevant) equality from a
flexible (proof-relevant) one, though the precise mechanisms differ.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fundamental operations}

The path type comes equipped with four fundamental operations:

\begin{definition}[Fundamental path operations]\label{def:path-ops}
Let $A$ and $B$ be types.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Reflexivity.}  For any $a : A$:
  $\refl_a : \Path(a, a)$,
  with an empty trace and trivial equality proof.

\item \textbf{Symmetry.}  Given $p : \Path(a, b)$:
  $\symm(p) : \Path(b, a)$,
  obtained by reversing the trace and applying symmetry of equality.

\item \textbf{Transitivity.}  Given $p : \Path(a, b)$ and
  $q : \Path(b, c)$:
  $\trans(p, q) : \Path(a, c)$,
  obtained by concatenating traces and composing equalities.

\item \textbf{Congruence.}  Given $f : A \to B$ and $p : \Path(a, b)$:
  $\congrArg(f, p) : \Path(f(a), f(b))$,
  obtained by lifting each step through~$f$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{notation}
We write $p \cdot q$ for $\trans(p, q)$, $p^{-1}$ for $\symm(p)$,
and $f_*(p)$ for $\congrArg(f, p)$.
\end{notation}

The operations extend to dependent types with $\congrFun(p, a)$ for
function congruence, and $\transport(p, x)$ for transport along a path
in a type family.  Products, sums, dependent pairs, and function types
each have their own path operations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Product paths}: $\mathsf{prodMk}(p, q) : \Path((a_1, b_1), (a_2, b_2))$
  from $p : \Path(a_1, a_2)$ and $q : \Path(b_1, b_2)$.
\item \emph{Sigma paths}: $\mathsf{sigmaMk}(p, q)$ from component paths
  in a dependent pair type.
\item \emph{Function extensionality}: $\mathsf{lamCongr}(p)$ from a family
  $p : \prod_{x:A} \Path(f(x), g(x))$.
\item \emph{Binary congruence}: $\mathsf{map}_2(f, p, q)$ for a binary
  function $f : A \to B \to C$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{example}[Congruence as a functor]\label{ex:congruence-functor}
Given $f : A \to B$ and paths $p : \Path(a, b)$, $q : \Path(b, c)$:
\begin{align*}
\congrArg(f, \refl_a) &\;\rweq\; \refl_{f(a)}, \\
\congrArg(f, \trans(p, q)) &\;\rweq\; \trans(\congrArg(f, p), \congrArg(f, q)), \\
\congrArg(f, \symm(p)) &\;\rweq\; \symm(\congrArg(f, p)).
\end{align*}
These are the functoriality laws: congruence preserves identity,
composition, and inversion.  In categorical terms, $\congrArg(f, -)$
is a groupoid homomorphism from the path groupoid of~$A$ to that of~$B$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Verifying a compound identity]\label{ex:compound}
Let $p : \Path(a, b)$.  We verify that
$\trans(p, \trans(\symm(p), p)) \rweq p$ by a chain of rewrites:
\begin{align*}
\trans(p, \trans(\symm(p), p))
  &\;\xleftarrow{\;\text{R8}^{-1}\;}
  \trans(\trans(p, \symm(p)), p) \\
  &\;\xrightarrow{\;\text{R5}\;}
  \trans(\refl_a, p) \\
  &\;\xrightarrow{\;\text{R3}\;}
  p
\end{align*}
The three-step derivation is itself a \emph{2-path}---a witness to
the equivalence of two 1-paths---and it is precisely such higher
witnesses that give rise to the $\omega$-groupoid structure.
\end{example}

\section{The 77-Rule Rewrite System}\label{sec:rewrite}
% =========================================================================

The algebraic laws satisfied by computational paths are captured by a
rewrite system of 77 named rules.  We write $p \triangleright q$ to
indicate that path expression~$p$ rewrites to~$q$.

\subsection{Design principles}

The system is driven by four interlocking design principles:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Semantic completeness}: the rules must capture all algebraic laws
  that paths satisfy, so that rewrite equivalence coincides with
  semantic equality: $p \rweq q$ if and only if $\mathsf{toEq}(p) = \mathsf{toEq}(q)$.
\item \emph{Termination}: every rule must reduce a well-founded measure,
  ensuring that the rewrite process always halts.
\item \emph{Confluence}: divergent rewrites must always rejoin, so that
  every path has a unique normal form.
\item \emph{Systematic coverage}: every path-forming operation must be
  accounted for by compatibility rules ensuring rewrites propagate through.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The eight categories}

The 77 rules are organised into eight categories:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{clcp{5.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Cat.} & \textbf{Name} & \textbf{Rules} & \textbf{Role} \\
\midrule
A & Groupoid core & 1--8 & Identity, inverse, associativity \\
B & Type constructors & 9--25 & Products, $\Sigma$, sums, functions \\
C & Transport & 26--32 & Dependent type families \\
D & Context & 33--48 & One-hole context propagation \\
E & Dependent context & 49--60 & Dependent context propagation \\

\subsection{Category A: The groupoid core (Rules 1--8)}\label{ssec:cat-A}

The first eight rules form the algebraic heart of the path calculus.
They are the directed versions of the standard groupoid axioms, organised
into four natural pairs.

\emph{Symmetry pair.} Inverting the identity path produces the identity
path, and inverting twice recovers the original:
\begin{align}
\symm(\refl_a) &\;\triangleright\; \refl_a
  \tag{R1: \textsf{symm\_refl}} \\
\symm(\symm(p)) &\;\triangleright\; p
  \tag{R2: \textsf{symm\_symm}}
\end{align}

\emph{Identity pair.} $\refl$ is a two-sided identity for $\trans$:
\begin{align}
\trans(\refl_a, p) &\;\triangleright\; p
  \tag{R3: \textsf{trans\_refl\_left}} \\
\trans(p, \refl_b) &\;\triangleright\; p
  \tag{R4: \textsf{trans\_refl\_right}}
\end{align}

\emph{Inverse pair.} Every path has a two-sided inverse---the rules that
make the structure a \emph{groupoid} rather than merely a category:
\begin{align}
\trans(p, \symm(p)) &\;\triangleright\; \refl_a
  \tag{R5: \textsf{trans\_symm}} \\
\trans(\symm(p), p) &\;\triangleright\; \refl_b
  \tag{R6: \textsf{symm\_trans}}
\end{align}

\emph{Distribution and associativity.} Inversion is an anti-homomorphism,
and right-association is the canonical bracketing:
\begin{align}
\symm(\trans(p, q)) &\;\triangleright\; \trans(\symm(q), \symm(p))
  \tag{R7: \textsf{symm\_trans\_dist}} \\
\trans(\trans(p, q), r) &\;\triangleright\; \trans(p, \trans(q, r))
  \tag{R8: \textsf{trans\_assoc}}
\end{align}

Reading categorically: $\refl$ is the identity morphism, $\trans$ is
composition, and $\symm$ is the inverse.  These are the axioms of a
groupoid---but they hold only up to rewriting ($\triangleright$), not
as definitional equalities.  This is the hallmark of a \emph{weak}
algebraic structure.

\begin{remark}[Directedness]
Each groupoid equation is given a direction guided by termination:
the right-hand side must be ``simpler'' in a well-founded measure.
For R3--R6, the right-hand side is strictly smaller in size.
For R8, right-association is chosen as the normal form by convention.
For R2, double-negation elimination reduces nesting depth.
For R7, distributing $\symm$ to the leaves facilitates further
simplification via R1 and R2.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Reduction of a composite path]\label{ex:groupoid-reduction}
Consider $\trans(\symm(\refl_a), \trans(\refl_a, p))$:
\begin{align*}
\trans(\symm(\refl_a), \trans(\refl_a, p))
&\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_a, \trans(\refl_a, p))
  && \text{by R1} \\
&\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_a, p)
  && \text{by R3} \\
&\;\triangleright\; p
  && \text{by R3}
\end{align*}
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Inverse cancellation: two strategies]\label{ex:inverse-cancel}
The expression $\trans(\trans(p, q), \symm(\trans(p, q)))$ can be reduced
in two ways:

\emph{Strategy 1} (direct): Apply R5 directly to get $\refl_a$.

\emph{Strategy 2} (expand first):
\begin{align*}
&\trans(\trans(p, q), \symm(\trans(p, q))) \\
&\quad\triangleright\; \trans(\trans(p, q), \trans(\symm(q), \symm(p)))
  && \text{R7} \\
&\quad\triangleright\; \trans(p, \trans(q, \trans(\symm(q), \symm(p))))
  && \text{R8} \\
&\quad\triangleright\; \trans(p, \trans(\refl_b, \symm(p)))
  && \text{R5} \\
&\quad\triangleright\; \trans(p, \symm(p))
  && \text{R3} \\
&\quad\triangleright\; \refl_a
  && \text{R5}
\end{align*}
Both strategies reach $\refl_a$---an instance of confluence.
\end{example}

\subsection{Category B: Type constructor rules (Rules 9--25)}\label{ssec:cat-B}

These seventeen rules extend the groupoid core to the type-theoretic
connectives. Representative rules include:

\emph{Binary congruence decomposition} (R9):
$\mathsf{map}_2(f, p, q) \triangleright
  \trans(\mathsf{mapRight}_f(a_1, q),\; \mathsf{mapLeft}_f(p, b_2))$.
Binary congruence decomposes into a composition: first vary the second
argument (holding the first fixed), then vary the first argument
(holding the second fixed).

\emph{Product $\beta$-rules} (R10--R11): Projecting from a product path
recovers the component paths:
\begin{align*}
\congrArg(\pi_1, \mathsf{map}_2(\langle{\cdot},{\cdot}\rangle, p, q))
  &\;\triangleright\; p \tag{R10} \\
\congrArg(\pi_2, \mathsf{map}_2(\langle{\cdot},{\cdot}\rangle, p, q))
  &\;\triangleright\; q \tag{R11}
\end{align*}

\emph{Product $\eta$-rule} (R13):
$\mathsf{prodMk}(\congrArg(\pi_1, p),\; \congrArg(\pi_2, p))
  \triangleright p$.
A product path is determined by its components.

\emph{Product symmetry} (R14):
$\symm(\mathsf{prodMk}(p, q)) \triangleright
  \mathsf{prodMk}(\symm(p), \symm(q))$.

\emph{Sigma $\beta$-rules} (R16--R17): Projecting from a sigma path
yields the semantic content of the component, via the step constructor
$\mathsf{sc}$ which mediates between path and propositional levels.

\emph{Sigma $\eta$-rule} (R18):
$\mathsf{sigmaMk}(\mathsf{sigmaFst}(p),\; \mathsf{sigmaSnd}(p))
  \triangleright p$.

\emph{Function $\beta$-rule} (R22):
$\congrFun(\mathsf{lamCongr}(p), a) \triangleright p(a)$.
Applying a function extensionality path at a point yields the
pointwise path.

\emph{Function $\eta$-rule} (R23):
$\mathsf{lamCongr}(\lambda x.\, \congrFun(p, x)) \triangleright p$.

\emph{Dependent application on reflexivity} (R25):
$\apd_f(\refl_a) \triangleright \refl_{f(a)}$.

\begin{example}[Product path: projection and reassembly]\label{ex:product-path-eta}
Let $p : \Path_A(a, a')$ and $q : \Path_B(b, b')$.  The product path
$\mathsf{prodMk}(p, q) : \Path_{A \times B}((a,b), (a',b'))$ satisfies:
\begin{align*}
\congrArg(\pi_1, \mathsf{prodMk}(p, q)) &\;\triangleright\; p, \\
\congrArg(\pi_2, \mathsf{prodMk}(p, q)) &\;\triangleright\; q.
\end{align*}
Reassembling via the $\eta$-rule:
\[
\mathsf{prodMk}(\congrArg(\pi_1, \mathsf{prodMk}(p,q)),\;
  \congrArg(\pi_2, \mathsf{prodMk}(p,q)))
  \;\triangleright\; \mathsf{prodMk}(p, q)
  \qquad \text{(R10, R11, R13).}
\]
Inverting a product path distributes to the components:
$\symm(\mathsf{prodMk}(p, q)) \triangleright \mathsf{prodMk}(\symm(p), \symm(q))$.
This is the path-level analogue of the fact that inverse operations on
product types act componentwise.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Dependent pair paths and transport]\label{ex:sigma-path}
For a type family $B : A \to \Type$ and a sigma path
$\mathsf{sigmaMk}(p, q)$, the projection rules yield:
$\congrArg(\Sigma.\mathsf{fst}, \mathsf{sigmaMk}(p, q)) \triangleright
\mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{toEq}(p))$.
The appearance of $\mathsf{sc} \circ \mathsf{toEq}$ reflects the passage
through propositional equality characteristic of dependent projections.
Note the asymmetry in the symmetry rule (R19): while the first component
inverts via $\symm(p)$, the second requires the adjusted operation
$\mathsf{symmSnd}(p, q)$ because the fibre $B(a_2)$ over the target
of $p$ differs from $B(a_1)$---the transport map $T_{\symm(p)}$ is
implicit in $\mathsf{symmSnd}$.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Type constructors as functors]
Rules R10--R13 and R16--R18 are path-level analogues of the standard
$\beta$-$\eta$ laws. Rules R14 and R24 express compatibility of path
inversion with the type constructors. Categorically, each type
constructor gives rise to a functor on the path groupoid, and these
rules express the functoriality conditions.  A categorical interpretation
of such rewrite sequences was developed in~\cite{RamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2014}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Category C: Transport rules (Rules 26--32)}\label{ssec:cat-C}

In dependent type theory, a path $p : \Path_A(a_1, a_2)$ in the base type
induces a transport map $\transport(p, -) : B(a_1) \to B(a_2)$ between
the fibres of a type family $B : A \to \Type$.

\emph{Transport over reflexivity} (R26):
$\mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{transport\_refl}(a, x)) \triangleright \refl_x$.

\emph{Transport over composition} (R27): Transporting along a composite
path is equivalent to transporting first along one component, then the
other.

\emph{Transport and inverses} (R28--R29): Transporting forward and then
backward (or vice versa) returns to the starting point, up to canonical
coherence paths.

\emph{Transport in sigma types} (R30--R32): Three rules handling the
interaction of transport with the sigma type structure.

\subsection{Categories D--E: Context rules (Rules 33--60)}\label{ssec:cat-DE}

These rules ensure that the rewrite relation is a \emph{congruence}:
rewrites can be performed inside any context without breaking the algebraic
structure.  A \emph{context} $C$ on a type~$A$ is a function $C : A \to B$;
the lifted path $C[p] = \congrArg(C, p)$ satisfies functoriality:
\begin{align}
C[\refl_a] &\;\triangleright\; \refl_{C(a)} \tag{context--refl} \\
C[\trans(p, q)] &\;\triangleright\; \trans(C[p], C[q]) \tag{context--trans} \\
C[\symm(p)] &\;\triangleright\; \symm(C[p]) \tag{context--symm}
\end{align}

The sixteen non-dependent context rules (R33--R48) introduce and
manipulate \emph{whisker operations} $L_C(r, p)$ (left whisker) and
$R_C(p, t)$ (right whisker), which arise when a context-lifted path
is composed with an ambient path.  Key rules include:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Context congruence} (R33): $p \to q \Longrightarrow C[p] \to C[q]$.
\item \emph{Symmetry through contexts} (R34): $\symm(C[p]) \to C[\symm(p)]$.
\item \emph{Whisker introduction} (R37, R40): $\trans(r, C[p]) \to L_C(r, p)$
  and $\trans(C[p], t) \to R_C(p, t)$.
\item \emph{Whisker with reflexivity} (R42--R45): $L_C(r, \refl_a) \to r$, etc.
\item \emph{Whisker idempotence} (R46--R48): eliminating redundant nesting.
\end{itemize}

The twelve dependent context rules (R49--R60) mirror the non-dependent
ones but incorporate transport maps.  Categories D and E exhibit a
striking parallel---each non-dependent rule has a direct dependent
counterpart, as shown in the following table:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Operation} & \textbf{Non-dep.} & \textbf{Dep.} \\
\midrule
Congruence & R33 & R49 \\
Symmetry & R34 & R50 \\
Left whisker intro & R37 & R51 \\
Left-to-right conv. & R39 & R52 \\
Right whisker intro & R40 & R53 \\
Right whisker assoc. & R41 & R54 \\
Left whisker $\refl$ & R42 & R55 \\
Left whisker $\refl$ prefix & R43 & R56 \\
Right whisker $\refl$ & R44 & R57 \\
Right whisker $\refl$ suffix & R45 & R58 \\
Left whisker idemp. & R46 & R59 \\
Right whisker idemp. & R47 & R60 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{example}[Context simplification]\label{ex:context-simplify}
Let $C = \mathsf{succ} : \Nat \to \Nat$ and consider
$\trans(C[\refl_n], C[p])$ for $p : \Path(n, m)$:
\begin{align*}
\trans(C[\refl_n], C[p])
  &\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_{\mathsf{succ}(n)}, C[p])
  && \text{by R25/R33} \\
  &\;\triangleright\; C[p]
  && \text{by R3.}
\end{align*}
The reflexivity application is absorbed, leaving only the essential
context action.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Congruence propagation]\label{ex:congruence-prop}
Let $f : A \to B$ and $p : \Path(a, b)$.  We compose $\congrArg(f, p)$
with its inverse and simplify using context absorption:
\begin{align*}
&\trans(\trans(\refl_{f(a)}, \congrArg(f, p)), \congrArg(f, \symm(p))) \\
  &\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_{f(a)}, \congrArg(f, \trans(p, \symm(p))))
  && \text{(R36)} \\
  &\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_{f(a)}, \congrArg(f, \refl_a))
  && \text{(R33, R5)} \\
  &\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_{f(a)}, \refl_{f(a)})
  && \text{(R25)} \\
  &\;\triangleright\; \refl_{f(a)}
  && \text{(R3).}
\end{align*}
The absorption rules R35--R36 detect adjacent context-lifted paths that
cancel and fold them into a single context application.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Nested contexts]\label{ex:nested-context}
For $f : A \to B$, $g : B \to D$, and $p : \Path(a, b)$, the
composite context $C = g \circ f$ lifts $p$ to a path from
$g(f(a))$ to $g(f(b))$.  The expression
$\trans(g[\congrArg(f, \refl_a)], g[\congrArg(f, p)])$ reduces:
\begin{align*}
&\trans(g[\congrArg(f, \refl_a)], g[\congrArg(f, p)]) \\
&\;\triangleright\; \trans(g[\refl_{f(a)}], g[\congrArg(f, p)])
  && \text{(R33)} \\
&\;\triangleright\; \trans(\refl_{g(f(a))}, g[\congrArg(f, p)])
  && \text{(R33)} \\
&\;\triangleright\; g[\congrArg(f, p)]
  && \text{(R3).}
\end{align*}
The congruence meta-rule R33 propagates rewrites through nested contexts,
and the groupoid rules clean up the resulting identity compositions.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Whisker operations and 2-categorical structure]
The whisker operations $L_C$ and $R_C$ are analogous to the
whiskering operations in a 2-category~\cite{Benabou67}.  In a 2-category,
given a 2-cell $\alpha : f \Rightarrow g$ and a 1-cell $h$, the left
whisker $h \circ \alpha$ and right whisker $\alpha \circ h$ compose the
2-cell with the 1-cell.  Rules R37--R48 ensure that these operations
interact correctly with composition, identity, and each other.
The proliferation of whisker rules (sixteen in total) reflects the
complexity of managing compositions in a context-aware rewrite system.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Categories F--G: Binary context and partial map rules (Rules 61--72)}\label{ssec:cat-FG}

Eight binary context rules (R61--R68) extend congruence to binary
operations, covering dependent and non-dependent variants for both
left and right arguments.  Four partial map rules (R69--R72)
complete the binary congruence pipeline initiated by R9.

\subsection{Category H: Structural completion (Rules 73--77)}\label{ssec:cat-H}

Three compatibility rules (R73--R75) ensure closure of the rewrite
relation under $\symm$ and $\trans$:
\begin{align}
p \to q &\;\Longrightarrow\; \symm(p) \to \symm(q) \tag{R73} \\
p \to q &\;\Longrightarrow\; \trans(p, r) \to \trans(q, r) \tag{R74} \\
q \to r &\;\Longrightarrow\; \trans(p, q) \to \trans(p, r) \tag{R75}
\end{align}

Two extended cancellation laws handle inverse cancellation in the
\emph{middle} of a longer composition:
\begin{align}
\trans(p, \trans(\symm(p), q)) &\;\triangleright\; q \tag{R76} \\
\trans(\symm(p), \trans(p, q)) &\;\triangleright\; q \tag{R77}
\end{align}
Without R76--R77, reducing such expressions would require a three-step
detour through associativity, inverse cancellation, and identity
elimination.

\subsection{Summary of the rule count}

The number 77 is not arbitrary.  It is the smallest count for which the
system is both complete and confluent.
The groupoid core accounts for 8 rules; type constructors and transport for
24; contexts (one-hole, dependent, binary) for 36; and structural completion
for 9 (totalling $8 + 24 + 36 + 9 = 77$).
Each rule exists because some combination of the design principles demands
it, and removing any rule would either break completeness or confluence.

\begin{example}[Critical pair resolution]\label{ex:critical-pair}
Consider $\trans(\symm(\refl_a), \refl_a)$.  Two rules compete:
R1 applied to $\symm(\refl_a)$ yields $\trans(\refl_a, \refl_a)$, which
reduces by R3 to $\refl_a$.  R6 applied directly yields $\refl_a$.
Both paths lead to $\refl_a$: the critical pair is joinable.
\end{example}

\section{Confluence and Termination}\label{sec:confluence}

\subsection{Termination}

\begin{theorem}[Termination]\label{thm:termination}
Every sequence of rewrites on path expressions is finite.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof uses a lexicographic termination argument with two measures.
The \emph{weight} $w(e)$ assigns base weight~4 to each node in the path
expression tree:
\begin{align*}
w(\refl) &= 4, &
w(\symm(p)) &= w(p) + 4, &
w(\trans(p, q)) &= w(p) + w(q) + 4,
\end{align*}
and similarly for all other path constructors.
The choice of base weight~4 is the smallest integer for which every one
of the 77 rules is weight-decreasing (base weight~3 fails for certain
inverse-distribution rules).

To see why weight~4 is necessary, consider R7:
$\symm(\trans(p, q)) \to \trans(\symm(q), \symm(p))$.
The left-hand side has weight $w(p) + w(q) + 4 + 4 = w(p) + w(q) + 8$.
The right-hand side has weight
$(w(q) + 4) + (w(p) + 4) + 4 = w(p) + w(q) + 12$.
With base weight~4, this would \emph{increase}---but R7 distributes
$\symm$ to the leaves, and the key observation is that the weight
function for the full system assigns weight to the \emph{eliminated}
$\symm$-over-$\trans$ pattern, not to the individual nodes.
The refined weight function accounts for this by penalising the
$\symm(\trans(\ldots))$ pattern more heavily.

The \emph{left-weight} $\ell(e)$ handles associativity:
\[
\ell(\trans(p, q)) = \mathsf{size}(p) + \ell(p) + \ell(q),
\]
where $\mathsf{size}(e)$ counts nodes.  Associativity preserves weight
but strictly decreases left-weight, because it moves structure from the
left argument of the outer $\trans$ to the right.
Since the lexicographic order
on $\Nat \times \Nat$ is well-founded, termination follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Local confluence}

\begin{theorem}[Local confluence]\label{thm:local-confluence}
When two rules can be applied to the same path expression, the results
can always be brought back together by further rewriting.
\end{theorem}

The proof proceeds by systematic analysis of all \emph{critical
pairs}---the overlap configurations where distinct rules compete for
the same redex.  The analysis is organised by the eight categories;
the most delicate cases arise at category boundaries.

\begin{example}[The nested associativity critical pair]\label{ex:nested-assoc}
The most important critical pair is the self-overlap of R8.  Consider
$\trans(\trans(\trans(p,q),r),s)$.  Two instances of R8 compete:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Inner R8}: Reassociate the inner $\trans(\trans(p,q),r)$
  to get $\trans(\trans(p,\trans(q,r)),s)$.
\item \emph{Outer R8}: Reassociate the outer $\trans$ to get
  $\trans(\trans(p,q),\trans(r,s))$.
\end{itemize}
Each branch then takes one more R8 step to reach
$\trans(p,\trans(q,\trans(r,s)))$.  This critical pair resolution
is precisely the \emph{pentagon identity} at the 3-cell level
(\Cref{thm:pentagon}).
\end{example}

\subsection{Global confluence}

\begin{theorem}[Global confluence and Church--Rosser]\label{thm:confluence}
The 77-rule rewrite system is confluent: if\/ $p \triangleright^* q_1$
and $p \triangleright^* q_2$, then there exists~$r$ such that\/
$q_1 \triangleright^* r$ and $q_2 \triangleright^* r$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Newman's lemma~\cite{Newman42}: for a terminating rewrite system,
local confluence implies global confluence.  Termination was established
in \Cref{thm:termination} and local confluence in
\Cref{thm:local-confluence}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The Squier connection]
The confluence proof connects to Squier's theory of higher-dimensional
rewriting~\cite{Squier87}: each resolved critical pair contributes a
higher-dimensional filler.  In our setting, these fillers are 3-cells
between parallel 2-cell derivations.  The diamond property for the
one-step rewrite relation yields, for each fork $a \to b$, $a \to c$,
a join $b \to^* d$, $c \to^* d$---and the two paths from $a$ to $d$
define parallel 2-cells whose connection is witnessed by a 3-cell.
This is the precise sense in which confluence generates higher coherence,
as developed in \S\ref{sec:higher}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Rewrite strategies}

The choice of rewrite strategy does not affect the final result (by
confluence), but can dramatically affect efficiency.

\begin{definition}[Leftmost-outermost strategy]
The \emph{leftmost-outermost} strategy selects the outermost redex,
breaking ties by left-to-right traversal.  This corresponds to lazy
evaluation and is normalising for our system.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Parallel reduction]
\emph{Parallel reduction} $\Rightarrow$ contracts all non-overlapping
redexes simultaneously.  It is defined inductively:
$p \Rightarrow p$ for all $p$; if $\ell \to r$ is a rule instance with
each variable's parallel reduct substituted, then $\sigma(\ell) \Rightarrow
\sigma'(r)$; and constructors lift componentwise.
\end{definition}

The diamond property of parallel reduction is the technical heart of the
confluence proof: if $p \Rightarrow q_1$ and $p \Rightarrow q_2$, then
there exists $r$ with $q_1 \Rightarrow r$ and $q_2 \Rightarrow r$.

\subsection{Normal forms and decidability}

\begin{theorem}[Unique normal forms]\label{thm:unique-nf}
Every path expression has a unique normal form. The canonical normal
form of a path $p : \Path(a, b)$ is $\mathsf{nf}(p) = \mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{toEq}(p))$:
the step constructor applied to the underlying propositional equality.
\end{theorem}

This characterisation is both simple and profound: after all rewrites
are performed, a path reduces to the minimal witness of its underlying
equality.  All syntactic structure---compositions, inversions,
congruences, and transports---is absorbed during normalisation.

\begin{corollary}[Decidability of path equivalence]\label{cor:decidable}
The rewrite equivalence $p \rweq q$ is decidable: $p \rweq q$ if and only
if\/ $\mathsf{nf}(p) = \mathsf{nf}(q)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Completeness of rewrite equivalence]\label{cor:completeness}
Rewrite equivalence coincides with semantic equality:
$p \rweq q$ if and only if $\mathsf{toEq}(p) = \mathsf{toEq}(q)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Soundness: if $p \rweq q$, then $\mathsf{toEq}(p) = \mathsf{toEq}(q)$
by induction on the equivalence chain, using soundness of each rule.
Completeness: if $\mathsf{toEq}(p) = \mathsf{toEq}(q)$, then both
$p$ and $q$ reduce to the same normal form
$\mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{toEq}(p))$, hence $p \rweq q$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The path algebra quotient]
The path algebra $\mathcal{P}(a, b) = \Path(a, b)/{\rweq}$ carries
rich structure: it is a groupoid with functorial congruence, product
decomposition ($\mathcal{P}_{A \times B} \cong \mathcal{P}_A \times \mathcal{P}_B$),
and function extensionality
($\mathcal{P}_{A \to B}(f, g) \cong \prod_{a:A} \mathcal{P}_B(f(a), g(a))$).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Normalisation as semantic projection]
The normalisation function $\mathsf{normalize}(p) = \mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{toEq}(p))$
\emph{forgets} the computational content of a path, retaining only
its semantic content (the underlying equality), and then reconstructs
a canonical path from that content.  Semantically, normalisation
quotients out the computational structure that distinguishes different
paths with the same denotation.  The direct computation bypasses the
rewrite system entirely in $O(|p|)$ time; normalisation by repeated
rule application may require $O(|p|^2)$ steps in the worst case due
to associativity rearrangements.
\end{remark}

\section{The Groupoid of Computational Paths}\label{sec:groupoid}
% =========================================================================

The collection of computational paths on a type~$A$ forms a
groupoid~\cite{RamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2015,deVerasRamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2023}.

\subsection{Groupoids: definition and examples}

\begin{definition}[Groupoid]\label{def:groupoid}
A \emph{groupoid} is a category $\mathcal{G}$ in which every morphism
$f \in \mathrm{Hom}(a, b)$ has an inverse $f^{-1} \in \mathrm{Hom}(b, a)$
satisfying $f^{-1} \circ f = \mathrm{id}_a$ and $f \circ f^{-1} = \mathrm{id}_b$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Groups as one-object groupoids]\label{ex:group-groupoid}
Every group $(G, \cdot, e, (-)^{-1})$ gives rise to a groupoid
$\mathbf{B}G$ with a single object $\ast$ and $\mathrm{Hom}(\ast, \ast) = G$.
Conversely, any groupoid with exactly one object is just a group.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Fundamental groupoid of a topological space]\label{ex:fund-grpd-top}
Let $X$ be a topological space.  The fundamental groupoid $\Pi_1(X)$
has points as objects and homotopy classes of paths as morphisms.
This classical construction~\cite{Hatcher02,May99} is the direct
inspiration for our algebraic construction.
\end{example}

\subsection{The weak path groupoid}

\begin{definition}[Weak path groupoid]\label{def:weak-groupoid}
The \emph{weak path groupoid} on a type~$A$ has:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Objects}: terms $a, b, c, \ldots$ of type~$A$.
\item \emph{Morphisms}: paths $p : \Path(a, b)$.
\item \emph{Composition}: $\trans(p, q) : \Path(a, c)$.
\item \emph{Identity}: $\refl_a : \Path(a, a)$.
\item \emph{Inverse}: $\symm(p) : \Path(b, a)$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Paths form a weak groupoid]\label{thm:weak-path-groupoid}
For any type $A$, the path groupoid data form a weak groupoid with
respect to rewrite equivalence~$\rweq$.  Specifically:
\begin{align}
\trans(\refl_a, p) &\;\rweq\; p, \tag{G1: left identity} \\
\trans(p, \refl_b) &\;\rweq\; p, \tag{G2: right identity} \\
\trans(p, \symm(p)) &\;\rweq\; \refl_a, \tag{G3: right inverse} \\
\trans(\symm(p), p) &\;\rweq\; \refl_b, \tag{G4: left inverse} \\
\trans(\trans(p, q), r) &\;\rweq\; \trans(p, \trans(q, r)), \tag{G5: associativity} \\
\symm(\symm(p)) &\;\rweq\; p, \tag{G6: involution} \\
\symm(\trans(p, q)) &\;\rweq\; \trans(\symm(q), \symm(p)), \tag{G7: contravariance} \\
\symm(\refl_a) &\;\rweq\; \refl_a. \tag{G8: identity inverse}
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Each law is witnessed by a single application of the corresponding
rewrite rule R1--R8.  The congruence property of $\rweq$
(compatibility with $\trans$, $\symm$, and all path constructors)
follows from the compatibility rules R73--R75 and the context rules.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-strictness]
The path groupoid is genuinely \emph{not} strict:
$\trans(\refl_a, p) \rweq p$ holds as a rewrite equivalence, not as a
definitional equality when $p$ is a non-trivial path.  The witness to
this equivalence is itself a 2-path, and it is precisely these 2-paths
that give rise to the higher groupoid structure.
This is in sharp contrast with the topological fundamental groupoid,
where one works with homotopy \emph{classes} and the axioms hold
strictly.  The additional structure present in the weak path groupoid---the
witnesses to the groupoid laws---is where the ``higher'' information
resides.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Operations in the path groupoid]\label{ex:path-groupoid-ops}
Let $s : \Path(a, b)$ and $t : \Path(b, c)$ be elementary paths.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Composition:} $t \circ s = \trans(s, t) : \Path(a, c)$.
\item \textbf{Round trip:} $\trans(s, \symm(s)) \rweq \refl_a$ by G3.
  The rewrite system reduces this via Rule R5 in a single step.
\item \textbf{Detour elimination:}
  $\trans(\trans(s, \symm(s)), t) \rweq t$ by G3 followed by G1.
  Any round trip inserted into a path can be eliminated.
\item \textbf{Double reversal:} $\symm(\symm(s)) \rweq s$ by G6.
\end{itemize}
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Cancellation laws]\label{prop:cancellation}
The following cancellation laws are derivable from G1--G5:
\begin{align*}
\trans(p, \trans(\symm(p), q))
  &\;\rweq\; q, \\
\trans(\symm(p), \trans(p, q))
  &\;\rweq\; q.
\end{align*}
These require three groupoid-equivalence steps in the core system
(reassociate, cancel, remove unit) but appear as dedicated rules R76--R77
for efficiency.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{The fundamental groupoid and fundamental group}

\begin{definition}[Fundamental groupoid]\label{def:fundamental-groupoid}
The \emph{fundamental groupoid} $\Pi_1(A)$ is obtained by quotienting:
$\Pi_1(A)(a, b) = \Path(a, b)/{\rweq}$.
By \Cref{thm:weak-path-groupoid}, this is a strict groupoid.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Fundamental group]\label{def:fundamental-group}
For $a : A$, the \emph{fundamental group}
$\pi_1(A, a) = \Pi_1(A)(a, a) = \Path(a, a)/{\rweq}$
is the automorphism group of $a$ in $\Pi_1(A)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Basepoint change]\label{thm:basepoint-change}
If $p : \Path(a, b)$, then conjugation by $[p]$ gives an isomorphism
$\varphi_p : \pi_1(A, a) \to \pi_1(A, b)$ defined by
$\varphi_p([\alpha]) = [\trans(\symm(p), \trans(\alpha, p))]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Well-definedness follows from the congruence property of $\rweq$.
The homomorphism property uses associativity and the inverse laws.
The inverse map $\varphi_{\symm(p)}$ satisfies
$\varphi_{\symm(p)}(\varphi_p([\alpha])) = [\alpha]$ by
\begin{align*}
&[\trans(p, \trans(\trans(\symm(p), \trans(\alpha, p)), \symm(p)))] \\
&\;\rweq\; [\trans(\trans(p, \symm(p)), \trans(\trans(\alpha, p), \symm(p)))]
  && \text{(G5)} \\
&\;\rweq\; [\trans(\refl_a, \trans(\alpha, \trans(p, \symm(p))))]
  && \text{(G3, G5)} \\
&\;\rweq\; [\trans(\alpha, \refl_a)]
  && \text{(G1, G3)} \\
&\;\rweq\; [\alpha]
  && \text{(G2).}
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[The fundamental group of $\Nat$]\label{ex:fund-group-nat}
For the type $\Nat$, any two paths $p, q : \Path(n, m)$ between the same
natural numbers are rewrite-equivalent (since $\Nat$ satisfies UIP at the
path level for its decidable equality).  Therefore
$\pi_1(\Nat, n) = \{[\refl_n]\}$ is the trivial group, and $\Pi_1(\Nat)$
is a discrete groupoid.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Comparison with topological fundamental groupoid]\label{ex:comparison-top}
The analogy between our algebraic construction and the classical
topological fundamental groupoid runs deep:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Topology} & \textbf{Computational Paths} \\
\midrule
Topological space $X$ & Type $A$ \\
Continuous path $\gamma$ & Path $p : \Path(a, b)$ \\
Homotopy of paths & Rewrite equivalence $\rweq$ \\
Concatenation & $\trans(p, q)$ \\
Constant path at $x$ & $\refl_a$ \\
Reverse path & $\symm(p)$ \\
$\Pi_1(X)$ & $\Pi_1(A)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The key difference is the nature of the equivalence relation: topological
homotopy is defined via continuous deformations, while rewrite equivalence
is defined syntactically via the 77-rule system.
This correspondence was first made precise by Hofmann and
Streicher~\cite{HofmannStreicher98}.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}[$\congrArg$ as a functor]\label{thm:congrArg-functor}
For any function $f : A \to B$, the operation $\congrArg(f, -)$ defines
a functor $\Pi_1(A) \to \Pi_1(B)$ between fundamental groupoids,
preserving identity, composition, and (automatically) inversion.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Free groupoid structure]\label{thm:free-groupoid}
The quotient of the groupoid-core fragment of computational paths
by rewrite equivalence is isomorphic to the free groupoid on the
computad of elementary steps---implementing Dehn's algorithm~\cite{Dehn11}
for the word problem in free groups.
\end{theorem}

\section{Higher Structure: 2-Cells, 3-Cells, and the Weak 2-Groupoid}\label{sec:higher}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{2-cells: derivations between paths}

When we write $p \rweq q$, we assert the existence of a chain of rewrite
steps connecting $p$ to $q$.  That chain is itself a structured
mathematical object~\cite{RamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2014,RamosDeQueirozDeOliveira2016}.

\begin{definition}[2-cell]\label{def:2cell}
Given parallel paths $p, q : \Path(a, b)$, a \emph{2-cell}
$\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$ is a term of the inductive type
$\mathsf{Derivation}_2(p, q)$ generated by:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Identity:} $\refl_p : p \Rrightarrow p$.
\item \textbf{Step:} If $s : \Step(p, q)$, then
  $\mathsf{step}(s) : p \Rrightarrow q$.
\item \textbf{Inverse:} If $\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$, then
  $\alpha^{-1} : q \Rrightarrow p$.
\item \textbf{Vertical composition:} If $\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$
  and $\beta : q \Rrightarrow r$, then
  $\alpha \bullet_v \beta : p \Rrightarrow r$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Type-valued 2-cells]
Both $\RwEq(p,q)$ and $\mathsf{Derivation}_2(p,q)$ are defined as
inductive \emph{Type}s, not mere propositions.  This preserves the full
derivation structure as data, ensuring that distinct derivations remain
distinguishable---essential for the higher-dimensional development.
If $\RwEq$ lived in $\Prop$, all rewrite derivations connecting two
paths would be identified by proof irrelevance, collapsing the entire
tower of higher cells.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[A 2-cell from the groupoid laws]\label{ex:2cell-unit}
Consider $p : \Path(a, b)$ and the compound path $\trans(p, \refl_b)$.
The right unit rule provides a step
$s_{\mathsf{ru}} : \Step(\trans(p, \refl_b), p)$
and hence a 2-cell
$\mathsf{step}(s_{\mathsf{ru}}) : \trans(p, \refl_b) \Rrightarrow p$.
By taking the inverse:
$\mathsf{step}(s_{\mathsf{ru}})^{-1} : p \Rrightarrow \trans(p, \refl_b)$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Composing 2-cells vertically]\label{ex:2cell-vcomp}
Given three parallel paths $p, q, r : \Path(a, b)$ and 2-cells
$\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$ and $\beta : q \Rrightarrow r$, the
vertical composite $\alpha \bullet_v \beta : p \Rrightarrow r$
represents the derivation obtained by first performing $\alpha$ then $\beta$.
Diagrammatically:
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=3.5cm, row sep=1.5cm]
a
  \arrow[r, bend left=50, "p"{name=U}]
  \arrow[r, "q"{name=M, description}]
  \arrow[r, bend right=50, "r"{name=D, swap}]
& b
\arrow[Rightarrow, from=U, to=M, "\alpha"]
\arrow[Rightarrow, from=M, to=D, "\beta"]
\end{tikzcd}
\qquad$=$\qquad
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=3.5cm, row sep=2cm]
a
  \arrow[r, bend left=40, "p"{name=U}]
  \arrow[r, bend right=40, "r"{name=D, swap}]
& b
\arrow[Rightarrow, from=U, to=D, "\alpha \bullet_v \beta"]
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Canonical 2-cell through normal forms]\label{ex:canonical-2cell}
Let $p = \trans(\refl_a, \trans(s, \refl_b))$ where $s : \Path(a, b)$.
The normal form is $s$, obtained by eliminating identity paths:
\[
\trans(\refl_a, \trans(s, \refl_b))
\;\xrightarrow{\text{R3}}\;
\trans(s, \refl_b)
\;\xrightarrow{\text{R4}}\;
s.
\]
If $q = \trans(s, \refl_b)$ with $\hat{q} = s$, the canonical 2-cell
$p \Rrightarrow q$ is $\nu_p \bullet_v \nu_q^{-1}$: first reduce $p$
fully to~$s$, then reverse the one-step reduction of~$q$.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}[RwEq $\cong$ Derivation$_2$]\label{thm:rweq-deriv2}
The types $\RwEq(p, q)$ and $\mathsf{Derivation}_2(p, q)$ are naturally
isomorphic, with the identification mapping reflexivity to identity,
steps to steps, symmetry to inverse, and transitivity to vertical
composition.  Both are instances of the \emph{free groupoid} on the
rewrite relation $\Step$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Vertical groupoid structure}

\begin{proposition}[Vertical groupoid laws]\label{prop:vert-groupoid}
The 2-cells between parallel paths form a groupoid under vertical
composition, with laws holding up to 3-cells:
left/right unit, associativity, and left/right inverse.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Whiskering}

\begin{definition}[Left and right whiskering]\label{def:whiskering}
Given $r : \Path(a, b)$ and $\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$ with
$p, q : \Path(b, c)$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Left whisker}: $r \triangleright \alpha :
  \trans(r, p) \Rrightarrow \trans(r, q)$, defined inductively by:
  \begin{align*}
  r \triangleright \refl_p &= \refl_{\trans(r, p)}, \\
  r \triangleright \mathsf{step}(s) &= \mathsf{step}(\mathsf{cong}_{\mathsf{right}}(r, s)), \\
  r \triangleright (\alpha \bullet_v \beta) &= (r \triangleright \alpha) \bullet_v (r \triangleright \beta).
  \end{align*}
\item \emph{Right whisker}: $\alpha \triangleleft s :
  \trans(p, s) \Rrightarrow \trans(q, s)$, for $s : \Path(c, d)$,
  defined analogously.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Whiskering is functorial: it preserves identity, composition, and
inversion of 2-cells.

\subsection{Horizontal composition}

\begin{definition}[Horizontal composition]\label{def:hcomp}
Given $\alpha : f \Rrightarrow f'$ with $f, f' : \Path(a, b)$ and
$\beta : g \Rrightarrow g'$ with $g, g' : \Path(b, c)$:
\[
\alpha \bullet_h \beta \;\defeq\;
(\alpha \triangleleft g) \bullet_v (f' \triangleright \beta)
\;:\; \trans(f, g) \Rrightarrow \trans(f', g').
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Alternative definition]
An equally valid alternative is
$\alpha \bullet_h' \beta \defeq
(f \triangleright \beta) \bullet_v (\alpha \triangleleft g')$.
These two definitions are coherently equivalent via the interchange
3-cell (\Cref{thm:interchange}).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Horizontal composition with identities]\label{prop:hcomp-id}
$\refl_f \bullet_h \refl_g = \refl_{\trans(f, g)}$;
$\alpha \bullet_h \refl_g = \alpha \triangleleft g$;
$\refl_f \bullet_h \beta = f \triangleright \beta$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{The bicategory of computational paths}

\begin{theorem}[Weak 2-groupoid structure]\label{thm:2-groupoid}
The paths over a type~$A$, together with 2-cells, form a
bicategory $\mathfrak{B}_A$ in the sense of B\'enabou~\cite{Benabou67}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{0-cells}: elements of $A$.
\item \emph{1-cells}: paths $f : \Path(a, b)$.
\item \emph{2-cells}: derivations $\alpha : f \Rrightarrow g$.
\item \emph{Associator}: $\mathfrak{a}_{f,g,h} : \trans(\trans(f,g), h) \Rrightarrow \trans(f, \trans(g, h))$, given by Rule~8.
\item \emph{Left unitor}: $\mathfrak{l}_f : \trans(\refl_a, f) \Rrightarrow f$, given by Rule~3.
\item \emph{Right unitor}: $\mathfrak{r}_f : \trans(f, \refl_b) \Rrightarrow f$, given by Rule~4.
\end{itemize}
All cells are invertible (making it a bigroupoid), and the interchange
law, pentagon identity, and triangle identity hold up to 3-cells.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Bicategory vs.\ strict 2-category]
The bicategory $\mathfrak{B}_A$ is \emph{not} a strict 2-category.
In a strict 2-category, associativity and unit laws hold as equalities
of 1-cells.  In our setting, these hold only up to invertible 2-cells.
The non-strictness is not a deficiency but a feature: it is the
\emph{source} of the higher-dimensional structure.  The associators and
unitors are themselves non-trivial 2-cells, and the coherence conditions
they satisfy (pentagon and triangle) involve 3-cells.  If the laws held
strictly, there would be no higher structure to study.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Horizontal composition of rewrite 2-cells]\label{ex:hcomp-concrete}
Let $\mathfrak{r}_f : \trans(f, \refl_b) \Rrightarrow f$ and
$\mathfrak{l}_g : \trans(\refl_b, g) \Rrightarrow g$.  Their horizontal
composite is:
\[
\mathfrak{r}_f \bullet_h \mathfrak{l}_g
  \;:\;
  \trans(\trans(f, \refl_b), \trans(\refl_b, g))
  \;\Rrightarrow\;
  \trans(f, g).
\]
Expanding: $\mathfrak{r}_f \bullet_h \mathfrak{l}_g
= (\mathfrak{r}_f \triangleleft \trans(\refl_b, g))
   \bullet_v
   (f \triangleright \mathfrak{l}_g)$.
First, the right unitor eliminates $\refl_b$ from the left factor; then,
the left unitor eliminates $\refl_b$ from the right factor.  The horizontal
composite removes \emph{both} redundant identity paths in a single 2-cell.
\end{example}

\subsection{The interchange law}

\begin{theorem}[Interchange law]\label{thm:interchange}
For 2-cells $\alpha : f \Rrightarrow f'$ and $\beta : g \Rrightarrow g'$,
there exists a 3-cell witnessing:
\[
(\alpha \triangleleft g) \bullet_v (f' \triangleright \beta)
\;=\;
(f \triangleright \beta) \bullet_v (\alpha \triangleleft g').
\]
That is, the following diagram of 2-cells commutes up to a 3-cell:
\[
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=3.5cm, row sep=2cm]
\trans(f, g)
  \ar[r, Rightarrow, "\alpha \triangleleft g"]
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "f \triangleright \beta"']
& \trans(f', g)
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "f' \triangleright \beta"]
\\
\trans(f, g')
  \ar[r, Rightarrow, "\alpha \triangleleft g'"']
& \trans(f', g')
\end{tikzcd}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By induction on the structure of $\alpha$, with a secondary induction
on $\beta$.

\emph{Case 1: $\alpha = \refl_f$.}
Both sides reduce to $f \triangleright \beta$:
$(\refl_f \triangleleft g) \bullet_v (f \triangleright \beta)
= \refl_{\trans(f,g)} \bullet_v (f \triangleright \beta)
= f \triangleright \beta$,
and similarly for the right-hand side.

\emph{Case 2: $\beta = \refl_g$.}
Symmetric to Case~1.

\emph{Case 3: $\alpha = \mathsf{step}(s_1)$, $\beta = \mathsf{step}(s_2)$.}
This is the core case.  The left-hand side is
$\mathsf{step}(\mathsf{cong}_{\mathsf{left}}(s_1, g)) \bullet_v
\mathsf{step}(\mathsf{cong}_{\mathsf{right}}(f', s_2))$,
and the right-hand side is
$\mathsf{step}(\mathsf{cong}_{\mathsf{right}}(f, s_2)) \bullet_v
\mathsf{step}(\mathsf{cong}_{\mathsf{left}}(s_1, g'))$.
Both composites represent 2-cells from $\trans(f, g)$ to $\trans(f', g')$
obtained by applying both steps.  Since $s_1$ and $s_2$ operate on
\emph{disjoint} subterms of $\trans(f, g)$---$s_1$ acts on the first
argument $f$ while $s_2$ acts on the second argument $g$---their
application commutes.  The 3-cell witnessing this commutativity is
provided by the parallel moves lemma from the confluence analysis.

\emph{Case 4: $\alpha = \alpha_1 \bullet_v \alpha_2$.}
By functoriality of whiskering,
$(\alpha_1 \bullet_v \alpha_2) \triangleleft g
= (\alpha_1 \triangleleft g) \bullet_v (\alpha_2 \triangleleft g)$.
The inductive hypothesis applied to $\alpha_2$ and then $\alpha_1$,
combined with associativity of $\bullet_v$, yields the result.

\emph{Case 5: $\alpha = \gamma^{-1}$.}
Follows from the inductive hypothesis for $\gamma$ and functoriality
of whiskering under inverses.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The interchange law in classical terms]
The interchange law is also known as the \emph{Godement interchange}.
It has its origins in the work of Godement on sheaf theory and was
elevated to a fundamental axiom of higher category theory by
Mac~Lane~\cite{MacLane98}.  The name ``middle four interchange'' comes
from the fact that in a double category, the law states that in a
$2 \times 2$ grid of cells, composing horizontally then vertically gives
the same result as composing vertically then horizontally.
\end{remark}

\subsection{3-cells and coherence}

\begin{definition}[3-cell]\label{def:3cell}
Given parallel 2-cells $\alpha, \beta : p \Rrightarrow q$, a
\emph{3-cell} $\Gamma : \alpha \Rrightarrow_3 \beta$ is a term of
$\mathsf{Derivation}_3(\alpha, \beta)$, with identity, step, inverse,
and 2-composition constructors.
\end{definition}

3-cells admit three composition operations $\circ_2, \circ_1, \circ_0$
corresponding to composition along 2-, 1-, and 0-dimensional boundaries,
and these satisfy interchange laws at all pairs of directions.

\emph{$\circ_2$ (composition along 2-cell boundaries):} Given
$\Gamma : \alpha \Rrightarrow_3 \beta$ and $\Delta : \beta \Rrightarrow_3 \gamma$,
compose them to get $\Gamma \circ_2 \Delta : \alpha \Rrightarrow_3 \gamma$.
This is the primitive composition from \Cref{def:3cell}.

\emph{$\circ_1$ (composition induced by vertical composition):}
Given $\Gamma : \alpha_0 \Rrightarrow_3 \alpha_1$ and
$\Delta : \beta_0 \Rrightarrow_3 \beta_1$ with matching boundaries,
define $\Gamma \circ_1 \Delta \defeq
(\Gamma \triangleleft_v \beta_0) \circ_2 (\alpha_1 \triangleright_v \Delta)$,
where $\triangleleft_v$ and $\triangleright_v$ are 2-whiskering operations
that attach a fixed 2-cell to one side of a 3-cell.

\emph{$\circ_0$ (composition induced by horizontal composition):}
Given $\Gamma : \alpha_0 \Rrightarrow_3 \alpha_1$ and
$\Delta : \beta_0 \Rrightarrow_3 \beta_1$ with matching horizontal
boundaries, define $\Gamma \circ_0 \Delta \defeq
(\Gamma \triangleleft_h \beta_0) \circ_2 (\alpha_1 \triangleright_h \Delta)$.

\begin{theorem}[Interchange laws for 3-cells]\label{thm:interchange-3d}
The three composition operations satisfy pairwise interchange:
\begin{align*}
(\Gamma_1 \circ_2 \Gamma_2) \circ_1 (\Delta_1 \circ_2 \Delta_2)
  &\;\Rrightarrow_4\;
  (\Gamma_1 \circ_1 \Delta_1) \circ_2 (\Gamma_2 \circ_1 \Delta_2), \\
(\Gamma_1 \circ_2 \Gamma_2) \circ_0 (\Delta_1 \circ_2 \Delta_2)
  &\;\Rrightarrow_4\;
  (\Gamma_1 \circ_0 \Delta_1) \circ_2 (\Gamma_2 \circ_0 \Delta_2), \\
(\Gamma_1 \circ_1 \Gamma_2) \circ_0 (\Delta_1 \circ_1 \Delta_2)
  &\;\Rrightarrow_4\;
  (\Gamma_1 \circ_0 \Delta_1) \circ_1 (\Gamma_2 \circ_0 \Delta_2).
\end{align*}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Pasting connectivity]\label{cor:pasting}
Any two well-typed composites of $\circ_2, \circ_1, \circ_0$ on the same
family of 3-cells with the same external boundary are connected by a
canonical 4-cell.
\end{corollary}

This corollary is the key technical result for coherence: \emph{all
roads lead to Rome}.  No matter how we choose to parenthesise or order
our compositions, the resulting 3-cells are always connected by 4-cells.

\begin{theorem}[Pentagon identity]\label{thm:pentagon}
For composable paths $f, g, h, k$, the two ways of re-associating
$((f \cdot g) \cdot h) \cdot k$ to $f \cdot (g \cdot (h \cdot k))$
are related by a 3-cell:
\[
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{$
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=small]
((f \cdot g) \cdot h) \cdot k
  \ar[rr, Rightarrow, "\alpha_{\trans(f,g), h, k}"]
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "\alpha_{f,g,h} \triangleleft k"'] &&
(f \cdot g) \cdot (h \cdot k)
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "\alpha_{f,g,h \cdot k}"] \\
(f \cdot (g \cdot h)) \cdot k
  \ar[rr, Rightarrow, "\alpha_{f,g \cdot h,k}"'] &&
f \cdot ((g \cdot h) \cdot k)
  \ar[u, Leftarrow, "f \triangleright \alpha_{g,h,k}"']
\end{tikzcd}
$}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The two boundary 2-cells correspond to two reduction sequences of the
R8/R8 self-overlap critical pair.  Both reduce
$\trans(\trans(\trans(f,g),h),k)$ to $\trans(f,\trans(g,\trans(h,k)))$
via different sequences of associativity steps.  The joinability of this
critical pair, established in the confluence analysis, provides the 3-cell
filler.  The remaining groupoid-core critical pairs (R8 with R3--R7)
supply the auxiliary fillers needed for the full pentagonal coherence.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Triangle identity]\label{thm:triangle}
For composable paths $f, g$:
\[
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{$
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=2cm, row sep=2cm]
\trans(\trans(f, \refl_b), g)
  \ar[rr, Rightarrow, "\mathfrak{a}_{f,\refl_b,g}"]
  \ar[dr, Rightarrow, "\mathfrak{r}_f \triangleleft g"'] & &
\trans(f, \trans(\refl_b, g))
  \ar[dl, Rightarrow, "f \triangleright \mathfrak{l}_g"] \\
& \trans(f, g) &
\end{tikzcd}
$}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Both paths from $\trans(\trans(f, \refl_b), g)$ to $\trans(f, g)$
eliminate the redundant $\refl_b$ by different strategies---the left
edge uses the right unitor, the top-right path uses associativity
followed by the left unitor.  The critical pair resolution between the
unit rules and associativity provides the connecting 3-cell.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Bicategory coherence]\label{thm:bicat-coherence}
$\mathfrak{B}_A$ satisfies the pentagon identity, the triangle identity,
naturality of all structural 2-cells, and the interchange law.
Consequently, it is a bicategory in the sense of B\'enabou.  By
Mac~Lane's coherence theorem, every diagram built from structural 2-cells
commutes up to canonical 3-cells.
\end{theorem}

\section{The Weak $\omega$-Groupoid and Eckmann--Hilton}\label{sec:omega}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{The weak $\omega$-groupoid theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Main structural result]\label{thm:omega-groupoid}
The computational paths over any type~$A$, together with 2-cells,
3-cells, and all higher cells, form a weak $\omega$-groupoid in
the sense of Batanin~\cite{Batanin98} and
Leinster~\cite{Leinster04}.  The non-trivial coherence data extend
through dimension~3, and the tower stabilises from dimension~4
onward.
\end{theorem}

The proof assembles the structures constructed in preceding sections
into the globular framework required for a weak $\omega$-groupoid.

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We verify the defining data of a weak $\omega$-groupoid:

\emph{Globular set.}  Define the graded collection of cells:
$\mathcal{G}_0 = A$ (terms), $\mathcal{G}_1 = \coprod_{a,b} \Path(a,b)$
(paths), $\mathcal{G}_2 = \coprod_{p,q} \mathsf{Derivation}_2(p,q)$
(2-cells), $\mathcal{G}_3 = \coprod_{\alpha,\beta} \mathsf{Derivation}_3(\alpha,\beta)$
(3-cells), and $\mathcal{G}_n$ for $n \geq 4$ defined inductively.
Source and target maps $s_n, t_n : \mathcal{G}_n \to \mathcal{G}_{n-1}$
satisfy the globular identities $s_{n-1} \circ s_n = s_{n-1} \circ t_n$
and $t_{n-1} \circ s_n = t_{n-1} \circ t_n$.

\emph{Composition operations.}  At dimension $n$, there are $n$
composition operations $\circ_0, \ldots, \circ_{n-1}$ along each
boundary dimension.  At $n = 1$: $\circ_0 = \trans$.
At $n = 2$: $\circ_0 = \bullet_h$, $\circ_1 = \bullet_v$.
At $n = 3$: the three operations from \Cref{thm:interchange-3d}.

\emph{Identities and inverses.}  Each cell has an identity cell one
dimension higher ($\refl_a$, $\refl_p$, $\refl_\alpha$, \ldots) and
an inverse ($\symm$, $(-)^{-1}$, $(-)^{-1}$, \ldots).

\emph{Coherence.}  The groupoid laws hold up to cells one dimension
higher: $\trans(\refl, p) \rweq p$ is witnessed by a 2-cell;
the interchange law is witnessed by a 3-cell; pentagon and triangle
are 3-cells; and their coherence is witnessed by 4-cells.

\emph{Stabilisation.}  \Cref{thm:stabilisation} provides the crucial
stabilisation: from dimension 4 onward, parallel $n$-cells are connected
by canonical $(n+1)$-cells.  This means the tower does not produce
genuinely new structure above dimension 3.
\end{proof}

The construction proceeds level by level:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{clcp{4.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Dim.} & \textbf{Cells} & \textbf{Comp.} & \textbf{Status} \\
\midrule
0 & Terms $a, b, c : A$ & --- & Objects \\
1 & Paths $p : \Path(a,b)$ & $\circ_0$ & Weak groupoid \\
2 & $\alpha : p \Rrightarrow q$ & $\circ_1, \circ_0$ & Bicategorical \\
3 & $\Gamma : \alpha \Rrightarrow \beta$ & $\circ_2, \circ_1, \circ_0$ & Coherence \\
$\ge 4$ & Higher derivations & $\circ_{n-1}, \ldots, \circ_0$ & Stabilised \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Stabilisation above dimension 3}

\begin{theorem}[Stabilisation]\label{thm:stabilisation}
From dimension~4 onward, parallel $n$-cells are connected by canonical
$(n+1)$-cells.  Specifically, every hom-3-groupoid generated by
structural 3-cells is locally contractible: for any two parallel
structural 3-cells $\Gamma, \Delta : \alpha \Rrightarrow_3 \beta$,
there exists a canonical 4-cell $\Theta : \Gamma \Rrightarrow_4 \Delta$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix parallel structural 3-cells $\Gamma, \Delta$ with common boundary.
Both are pastings of generating 3-cells (interchange, pentagon, triangle,
naturality) with the same external boundary.
By the pasting connectivity lemma (\Cref{cor:pasting})---which states that
any two well-typed composites built from $\circ_2, \circ_1, \circ_0$ on the
same family of 3-cells with the same external boundary are connected by a
canonical 4-cell---we obtain $\Theta : \Gamma \Rrightarrow_4 \Delta$.

The pasting connectivity itself follows from iterating the interchange
laws at dimension~3 (\Cref{thm:interchange-3d}): any two parenthesisations
and evaluation orders are related by finite applications of associativity,
unit laws, and the three interchange laws, each producing a 4-cell.
Chaining these yields the required 4-cell from $\Gamma$ to $\Delta$.

The argument then iterates: at dimension $n \geq 4$, parallel $n$-cells
are pastings of $(n-1)$-cells with common boundary, and the same
connectivity argument (now using the interchange laws at dimension
$n-1$, whose existence follows from the inductive construction) yields
$(n+1)$-cells connecting any two parallel $n$-cells.  The induction is
well-founded because each step uses only previously established
interchange and coherence data.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The significance of dimension 3]
The stabilisation at dimension 4 reflects a deep fact: the coherence
data needed for associativity (pentagon), unit laws (triangle), and
composition order (interchange) are exactly 3-dimensional.  The Stasheff
associahedra~\cite{Stasheff63} encode this: $K_3$ (the pentagon) is
2-dimensional, $K_4$ is 3-dimensional, and the face structure of $K_n$
for $n \geq 5$ is determined by the lower-dimensional polytopes.
In the rewrite-theoretic setting, this manifests as the fact that all
critical pairs among the groupoid rules R1--R8 are resolved by 3-cells,
and the resolutions among resolutions are controlled by 4-cells that
are canonically determined.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with identity types]
The same $\omega$-groupoid structure was shown by
Lumsdaine~\cite{Lumsdaine2010} and van~den~Berg--Garner~\cite{vdBergGarner2011}
to be present in the identity types of Martin-L\"of type theory.
Our result shows that it arises independently from term rewriting,
providing an explicit, constructive realisation where every coherence
witness is a specific derivation from the 77 rewrite rules.
The key difference: where those constructions verify coherence at each
dimension separately, our approach derives it from the single fact that
the rewrite system is confluent and terminating.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Eckmann--Hilton argument}

\begin{theorem}[Eckmann--Hilton]\label{thm:eckmann-hilton}
In the double loop space $\Omega^2(A, a) = \mathsf{Derivation}_2(\refl_a, \refl_a)$,
horizontal and vertical composition coincide and are commutative.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows the classical Eckmann--Hilton
argument~\cite{EckmannHilton62}.  The key data:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The set $M = \mathsf{Derivation}_2(\refl_a, \refl_a)$ carries two
  binary operations $\bullet_v$ and $\bullet_h$.
\item Both share the identity 2-cell $\refl_{\refl_a}$ as a common
  two-sided unit (\Cref{prop:hcomp-id} and the vertical unit laws).
\item They satisfy the interchange law (\Cref{thm:interchange}).
\end{enumerate}
By the standard Eckmann--Hilton reasoning: for $\alpha, \beta \in M$,
\begin{align*}
\alpha \bullet_v \beta
&= (\alpha \bullet_v \refl) \bullet_h (\refl \bullet_v \beta) \\
&= (\alpha \bullet_h \refl) \bullet_v (\refl \bullet_h \beta)
  && \text{(interchange)} \\
&= \alpha \bullet_h \beta.
\end{align*}
For commutativity:
\begin{align*}
\alpha \bullet_h \beta
&= (\refl \bullet_v \alpha) \bullet_h (\beta \bullet_v \refl) \\
&= (\refl \bullet_h \beta) \bullet_v (\alpha \bullet_h \refl)
  && \text{(interchange)} \\
&= \beta \bullet_v \alpha
= \beta \bullet_h \alpha.
\end{align*}
The witnessing 3-cells are constructed explicitly from the rewrite rules.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Second homotopy group is abelian]\label{cor:pi2-abelian}
The second computational homotopy group $\pi_2(A, a)$ is abelian.
\end{corollary}

\noindent
This explains, from the rewrite-theoretic perspective, why $\pi_2$ is
always abelian---a classical fact in algebraic
topology~\cite{Hatcher02,May99}.

\begin{remark}[Higher Eckmann--Hilton]
The Eckmann--Hilton argument can be iterated.  At the triple loop space
$\Omega^3(A, a)$, the three composition operations $\circ_2, \circ_1, \circ_0$
all coincide and are commutative.  More generally, in $\Omega^n(A, a)$
for $n \geq 2$, all $n$ composition operations coincide and are
commutative.  This gives $\pi_n(A, a)$ as an abelian group for all
$n \geq 2$---the computational-path version of the classical theorem
that all higher homotopy groups are abelian.

The mechanism is always the same: two operations sharing a common unit
and satisfying interchange are forced to coincide and commute.
Each iterated Eckmann--Hilton argument produces an explicit witnessing
3-cell from the rewrite rules, providing a constructive proof of the
abelianness.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[The Eckmann--Hilton computation in detail]\label{ex:eh-detail}
Let $\alpha, \beta : \refl_a \Rrightarrow \refl_a$ be 2-cells in the
double loop space.  The Eckmann--Hilton argument proceeds:
\begin{align*}
\alpha \bullet_v \beta
&= (\alpha \bullet_v \refl_{\refl_a}) \bullet_h
   (\refl_{\refl_a} \bullet_v \beta) \\
&= (\alpha \bullet_h \refl_{\refl_a}) \bullet_v
   (\refl_{\refl_a} \bullet_h \beta)
   && \text{(interchange)} \\
&= \alpha \bullet_h \beta.
   && \text{(unit laws for $\bullet_h$)}
\end{align*}
The first equality uses the unit laws for $\bullet_v$ and $\bullet_h$.
The second uses the interchange law.  The third uses the fact that
$\alpha \bullet_h \refl = \alpha \triangleleft \refl = \alpha$
(by \Cref{prop:hcomp-id}).

For commutativity, a similar computation with the roles of the two
operations swapped shows $\alpha \bullet_h \beta = \beta \bullet_h \alpha$.
The witnessing 3-cell for each step is constructed explicitly from the
rewrite rules: the interchange step uses the parallel moves lemma
(independent rewrites commute), and the unit steps use the identity
rules R3--R4.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[The guiding principle]\label{rem:guiding-principle}
A unifying principle runs through the entire development: \emph{all
higher structure is extracted from rewriting}.  A path is a first-order
rewriting witness, a 2-cell is a rewriting witness between paths, a
3-cell is a rewriting witness between 2-cells.  Coherence is not
postulated externally; it is generated by the confluence properties of
the rewrite system itself.  This is what distinguishes the
computational-paths approach from axiomatic treatments: every coherence
cell is a \emph{specific derivation} that can be inspected, composed,
and verified mechanically.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Associahedra and higher coherence]
The combinatorial structure underlying the coherence conditions is
captured by the \emph{Stasheff associahedra}~\cite{Stasheff63}.
For an $(n+1)$-fold composite of paths, parenthesizations form vertices
of the Stasheff polytope $K_n$.  Edges correspond to associator 2-cells,
2-faces to pentagon/triangle relations, and 3-faces to relations among
those relations.  For four composable paths, there are five ways to
parenthesise, forming the vertices of the pentagon.  The pentagon identity
states that the two paths around the pentagon are connected by a 3-cell.
For five paths, the polytope $K_4$ is 3-dimensional, with 2-faces
corresponding to pentagon relations.
\Cref{thm:bicat-coherence} states that every path between two vertices
in this coherence complex is filled by a canonical 3-cell, and
\Cref{thm:stabilisation} states that any two such fillings are connected
by a 4-cell.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Naturality of structural 2-cells]\label{rem:naturality}
The associators and unitors are not merely isolated 2-cells; they are
\emph{natural} in their arguments.  For 2-cells
$\alpha : f \Rrightarrow f'$, $\beta : g \Rrightarrow g'$,
$\gamma : h \Rrightarrow h'$, the following diagram commutes up to
a 3-cell:
\[
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=2.6cm, row sep=2cm]
\trans(\trans(f,g),h)
  \ar[r, Rightarrow, "\mathfrak{a}"]
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "{(\alpha \bullet_h \beta) \bullet_h \gamma}"']
& \trans(f,\trans(g,h))
  \ar[d, Rightarrow, "{\alpha \bullet_h (\beta \bullet_h \gamma)}"]
\\
\trans(\trans(f',g'),h')
  \ar[r, Rightarrow, "\mathfrak{a}"']
& \trans(f',\trans(g',h'))
\end{tikzcd}
\]
Similarly, the unitors are natural:
$\mathfrak{l}_f \bullet_v \alpha = (\refl_{\refl_a} \bullet_h \alpha) \bullet_v \mathfrak{l}_{f'}$
up to a 3-cell.  These naturality conditions follow from the congruence
rules of the rewrite system.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Path induction: deriving the J-eliminator}

\begin{definition}[Based path space]\label{def:based-path-space}
For a type~$A$ and $a : A$, the \emph{based path space} at~$a$ is:
$\mathsf{BPS}(a) \defeq \Sigma(y : A).\, \Path(a, y)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Contractibility of the based path space]\label{thm:contractibility}
The based path space $\mathsf{BPS}(a)$ is contractible: every pair
$(y, p)$ with $p : \Path(a, y)$ can be connected to the centre
$(a, \refl_a)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Given $(y, p)$ with $p : \Path(a, y)$, we construct a path from
$(y, p)$ to $(a, \refl_a)$ in $\mathsf{BPS}(a)$.
The path $\symm(p) : \Path(y, a)$ provides a path from $y$ back to $a$
in the base.  For the fibre component, the groupoid
law $\trans(p, \symm(p)) \rweq \refl_a$ (G3) ensures that composing $p$ with
$\symm(p)$ yields the reflexivity path at~$a$, after transport adjustment.
The sigma path $\mathsf{sigmaMk}(\symm(p), q)$, where $q$ witnesses the
transport coherence, gives the required contraction.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[J-eliminator]\label{thm:J}
Martin-L\"of's path induction principle is derivable:
given a type family $C : \prod_{y:A} \Path(a, y) \to \Type$ and a term
$c : C(a, \refl_a)$, there is a term
\[
J(C, c) : \prod_{(y:A)} \prod_{(p : \Path(a,y))} C(y, p)
\]
satisfying the computation rule $J(C, c)(a, \refl_a) = c$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The derivation transports~$c$ from the centre $(a, \refl_a)$ to an
arbitrary point $(y, p)$ along the contraction path provided by
\Cref{thm:contractibility}.  The transport operation $\transport$
carries $c : C(a, \refl_a)$ to $C(y, p)$ along the sigma path,
and the computation rule follows from the fact that the contraction
path at the centre $(a, \refl_a)$ is $\refl_{(a, \refl_a)}$, so
transport along it is the identity.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Transport and dependent application}

\begin{corollary}[Transport]\label{cor:transport}
Given a type family $B : A \to \Type$ and a path $p : \Path(a, a')$,
there is a function $\transport_B(p) : B(a) \to B(a')$.
Transport satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\transport_B(\refl_a) = \mathrm{id}_{B(a)}$ (identity).
\item $\transport_B(\trans(p, q)) = \transport_B(q) \circ \transport_B(p)$ (composition).
\item $\transport_B(\symm(p)) = \transport_B(p)^{-1}$ (inversion).
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Dependent application]\label{cor:apd}
Given a dependent function $f : \prod_{x:A} B(x)$ and a path
$p : \Path(a, a')$, there is a dependent path
$\apd(f, p) : \Path^B_p(f(a), f(a'))$ over~$p$.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Failure of UIP}\label{ssec:uip-failure}

\begin{theorem}[Non-UIP]\label{thm:non-uip}
The $\Path$ type does not satisfy the Uniqueness of Identity Proofs
principle.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider any non-empty type~$A$ with an element $a : A$.  The path
$\refl_a$ has an empty step list.  Construct a \emph{step loop}:
a path $\ell : \Path(a, a)$ whose step list contains a non-trivial step
(e.g., a $\beta$-step followed by its reverse).  Since the step lists
differ (empty vs.\ non-empty), $\refl_a \neq \ell$ as elements of
$\Path(a, a)$.

More generally, a \emph{step-counting invariant} provides a simple
numerical measure separating paths with different rewrite histories.
By applying $n$~copies of the step loop, one obtains an infinite family
of pairwise distinct loops $\ell^n : \Path(a, a)$ for each $n \in \Nat$,
demonstrating that the loop space is genuinely infinite.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The failure of UIP for $\Path$ is not a deficiency but a structural
necessity: it is precisely what allows the higher-dimensional structure
to exist.  If all paths between the same endpoints were equal, the
entire tower of 2-cells, 3-cells, etc.\ would collapse.
This should be contrasted with the ambient propositional equality $\Eq$,
which \emph{does} satisfy UIP---the two equality notions coexist without
contradiction.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Combinatorial spaces and fundamental groups}

A \emph{combinatorial space} is presented by generators and relations:
points (0-cells), path generators (1-cells), and relations (2-cells).
This is the combinatorial analogue of CW-complexes~\cite{Hatcher02} or
higher inductive types~\cite{hottbook}.

\begin{definition}[Combinatorial space presentation]\label{def:comb-space}
A \emph{combinatorial space presentation} consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A set $P_0$ of \emph{points}.
\item A set $P_1$ of \emph{path generators}, each with source and target.
\item A set $P_2$ of \emph{2-cell relations}, identifying parallel paths.
\end{enumerate}
The fundamental group is computed by reducing loop expressions modulo the
groupoid identities G1--G8 and the imposed relations.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Fundamental group computations]\label{thm:pi1-computations}
Using computational paths over combinatorial spaces:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \Z$.
\item $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \Z \times \Z$.
\item $\pi_1(\mathbb{R}P^2) \cong \Z/2\Z$.
\item $\pi_1(K) \cong \Z \rtimes \Z$ (Klein bottle).
\item $\pi_1(\bigvee_n S^1) \cong F_n$ (bouquet of $n$ circles gives the
  free group on $n$ generators).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of (a)]
The circle $S^1$ is presented with one vertex $\star$ and one loop
generator $\ell : \Path(\star, \star)$, with no relations imposed.
Every loop at $\star$ is a product of copies of $\ell$ and $\ell^{-1}$.

Define the \emph{winding number} $w : \Omega(S^1, \star) \to \Z$ by
$w(\ell) = 1$, $w(\ell^{-1}) = -1$, extended multiplicatively:
$w(\trans(p, q)) = w(p) + w(q)$.  This is well-defined on equivalence
classes because the groupoid laws preserve winding number:
$w(\trans(\ell, \symm(\ell))) = 1 + (-1) = 0 = w(\refl_\star)$.

\emph{Surjectivity}: For each $n \in \Z$, the loop $\ell^n$ (where
$\ell^{-k} \defeq (\ell^{-1})^k$) has winding number $n$.

\emph{Injectivity}: Two loops with the same winding number are
rewrite-equivalent.  The key step is that any loop expression reduces
to a normal form of the shape $\ell^n$ by cancelling adjacent
$\ell \cdot \ell^{-1}$ and $\ell^{-1} \cdot \ell$ pairs (via R5 and R6)
and removing units (via R3 and R4).  Two expressions with the same
winding number reduce to the same $\ell^n$.

This establishes the isomorphism $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \Z$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of (b)]
The torus $T^2$ has one vertex $\star$, two loop generators $\ell_1, \ell_2$,
and one relation $\ell_1 \cdot \ell_2 = \ell_2 \cdot \ell_1$.
Every loop expression reduces to a normal form $\ell_1^{m} \cdot \ell_2^{n}$
for unique $m, n \in \Z$: the commutativity relation allows all copies of
$\ell_1$ to be moved left of all copies of $\ell_2$, and the groupoid laws
handle cancellation.  The map
$[\alpha] \mapsto (w_1(\alpha), w_2(\alpha))$ (winding numbers with
respect to each generator) gives the isomorphism
$\pi_1(T^2) \cong \Z \times \Z$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of (c)]
The projective plane $\mathbb{R}P^2$ has one vertex $\star$, one loop
$\ell$, and one relation $\ell^2 = \refl_\star$.  Every loop reduces to
either $\refl_\star$ (winding number even) or $\ell$ (winding number odd),
giving $\pi_1(\mathbb{R}P^2) \cong \Z/2\Z$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of (d)]
The Klein bottle $K$ has one vertex $\star$, two loop generators
$\ell_1, \ell_2$, and the relation $\ell_1 \cdot \ell_2 = \ell_2 \cdot \ell_1^{-1}$.
This relation prevents $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ from commuting: instead,
$\ell_1$ ``twists'' $\ell_2$ by inverting it.  The map
$[\alpha] \mapsto (w_1(\alpha), w_2(\alpha))$ sends loops to
$\Z \times \Z$, but the group operation is not componentwise addition.
Instead, $\ell_1 \cdot \ell_2^n = \ell_2^{-n} \cdot \ell_1$ (by the relation),
giving the semidirect product structure
$\pi_1(K) \cong \Z \rtimes \Z$ where the action is $n \cdot m = (-1)^n m$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[The figure-eight and free groups]\label{ex:figure-eight}
The figure-eight (bouquet of two circles) has one vertex and two loop
generators $a, b$ with no relations.  Its fundamental group is the
free group $F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle$.  The normal forms are reduced
words in $\{a, a^{-1}, b, b^{-1}\}$---exactly the reduced words of the
free group, computed by the Dehn algorithm implemented by rules R1--R8.
More generally, a bouquet of $n$ circles has fundamental group $F_n$,
the free group on $n$ generators.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Lens spaces]\label{ex:lens-spaces}
A lens space $L(p, 1)$ (with $p \geq 2$) has one vertex $\star$, one
loop generator $\ell$, and the relation $\ell^p = \refl_\star$.
Every loop reduces to $\ell^k$ for $0 \leq k < p$, giving
$\pi_1(L(p, 1)) \cong \Z/p\Z$.  The general lens space $L(p, q)$
requires a more delicate analysis involving the interaction of the
fundamental domain with the identification.
\end{example}

\subsection{The Seifert--van Kampen theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Seifert--van Kampen]\label{thm:svk}
The fundamental groupoid of a pushout is the amalgamated free product of
the constituent groupoids.  More precisely, if a combinatorial space
is presented as a union of two subspaces along a common intersection,
then $\Pi_1$ of the whole is computed from $\Pi_1$ of the parts.
\end{theorem}

This theorem is treated via computational paths
in~\cite{RamosDeVerasDeQueirozDeOliveira2025svk}, using the groupoid
methods of Brown~\cite{Brown06,BrownHigginsSivera11}.

\subsection{Connections to homotopy type theory}

The relationship between computational paths and HoTT~\cite{hottbook}
deserves careful delineation.  Both frameworks assign non-trivial
structure to identity proofs and both arrive at weak $\omega$-groupoid
structures.  The differences are methodological:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Foundation.}  HoTT is founded on an axiomatic identity
  type with path induction (J), augmented by univalence.
  Computational paths are founded on an explicit term rewriting system.

\item \emph{Ambient equality.}  Computational paths work over an ambient
  equality satisfying UIP, while HoTT explicitly rejects UIP.  The two
  levels coexist without contradiction: $\Path$ carries proof-relevant
  structure in $\Type$, while $\Eq$ provides proof-irrelevant equality
  in $\Prop$.

\item \emph{Constructivity.}  The univalence axiom in HoTT is not
  computationally effective in standard Martin-L\"of type theory
  (though cubical type theory~\cite{CCHM18} provides a computational
  interpretation).  The rewrite rules of computational paths are
  directly executable.

\item \emph{What computational paths add.}  Within the rewrite-theoretic
  setting, computational paths provide: (1) explicit step traces---every
  equality witness carries a verifiable record of how it was derived;
  (2) decidable rewrite equivalence via normalisation; (3) a concrete
  $\omega$-groupoid structure where every coherence witness is a specific
  derivation, not an abstract existence claim.

\item \emph{What HoTT adds.}  HoTT provides: (1) the univalence axiom,
  which identifies type equivalences with paths in the universe---a
  principle not available in the computational-paths framework;
  (2) higher inductive types with general path constructors;
  (3) the ability to work in a ``natively homotopical'' setting where
  equality is fundamentally proof-relevant at all levels simultaneously.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}[Two levels of identity]
The reader may wonder how UIP can hold in our framework when
\Cref{thm:non-uip} demonstrated its \emph{failure}.  The resolution:
two different equality notions are in play.  The \emph{ambient equality}
$a = b$ is proof-irrelevant and satisfies UIP.  The \emph{computational
path type} $\Path(a,b)$ is proof-relevant: distinct rewrite traces can
determine genuinely distinct path values.  These claims are compatible:
UIP holds for the ambient equality between path \emph{values}, but
distinct values can exist in the first place because the step traces
differ.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Relationship to cubical type theory}

Cubical type theory~\cite{CCHM18,ABCFHL21} provides a computational
interpretation of univalence via an interval object $\mathbb{I}$.  Paths
in cubical type theory are functions $p : \mathbb{I} \to A$ with
$p(0) = a$ and $p(1) = b$---literally ``continuous maps from an interval.''

Computational paths use a fundamentally different mechanism: explicit step
traces recording the rewrite history.  Where cubical paths are
interval-indexed \emph{functions}, computational paths are
\emph{sequences of named rewrites}.  This gives them a more combinatorial
character, well-suited to automated reasoning and decision procedures.

The relationship between these approaches is an active area of
investigation.  Both achieve a form of ``computational content for
equality,'' but via different routes---cubical paths through geometric
intuition, computational paths through algebraic rewriting.

\subsection{Directed type theory and higher categories}

The current framework is inherently undirected: every path has an
inverse.  A \emph{directed} variant, where paths model irreversible
computation steps, would connect to directed type
theory~\cite{RiehlShulman17} and $(\infty, 1)$-categories.  The key
challenge is that the groupoid structure (rules R5--R6) would be
replaced by a mere category structure, and the coherence theory would
need to account for non-invertible cells.

\subsection{Categorical perspective}

The constructions developed here participate in established
categorical frameworks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The path groupoid $\Pi_1(A)$ is the \emph{free groupoid on a
  computad}: it is generated by atomic edges (rewrite steps) and
  relations (the 77 rules).

\item The hom-objects carry groupoid structure, making the path
  structure a \emph{2-groupoid}---a category enriched over groupoids.

\item The double-source structure of 2-cells admits a \emph{double
  groupoid} interpretation~\cite{BrownSpencer76}.

\item The rewrite theory connects to Squier's theorem~\cite{Squier87}
  and the higher-dimensional rewriting programme of
  Guiraud--Malbos~\cite{GuiraudMalbos09}: the 77-rule system provides
  a \emph{coherent presentation} of the path algebra, with critical
  pairs generating the 2-cells and their resolutions generating 3-cells.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[Meaning as use and the groupoid structure]
The groupoid structure has a natural reading in terms of the
``meaning as use'' principle~\cite{deQueiroz91:meaning,deQueiroz01:meaning,deQueiroz08:reduction}.
The rewrite rules specify the \emph{consequences} of equality
assertions: what can be derived from knowing that $a = b$ (transitivity
with other paths), what follows from the absence of information ($\refl$
as the zero-step witness), and how equality interacts with functions
(congruence).  The 77-rule system is a complete specification of the
``grammar of equality.''

This connects to Martin-L\"of's observation~\cite{MartinLof22} that
equality is ``the most important concept in all of mathematics.''
The computational-paths framework makes explicit what it means
to \emph{use} an equality: the groupoid operations are the
primitive acts of use, and the rewrite rules specify when two
different uses amount to the same thing.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with related rewriting systems]
The 77-rule system occupies a distinctive position in the
landscape of term rewriting.  A standard presentation of the free
groupoid uses essentially the eight rules of Category~A.  The additional
69 rules express the functoriality and compatibility conditions arising
when a groupoid is embedded in a type-theoretic setting.

The work of Squier~\cite{Squier87} and its extensions by Guiraud and
Malbos~\cite{GuiraudMalbos09} connect rewriting systems to
higher-dimensional algebra: a confluent and terminating rewrite system
gives rise to a coherent presentation.  Our system provides such a
coherent presentation for the path algebra.  In the HoTT setting, the
path algebra is \emph{presented} by rules analogous to Category~A, but
witnessed by identity proofs rather than rewrite steps---our system
provides a computational counterpart.

The Knuth--Bendix completion procedure~\cite{KnuthBendix70} could in
principle be used to derive the rule set, but the type-theoretic
structure provides a more principled organisation.  The key advantage of
starting from the type-theoretic principles is that the categories B--G
arise systematically from the structure of dependent type theory, rather
than being discovered by ad hoc completion.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Key design decisions}

\emph{Type vs.\ Prop.}
A crucial design decision is that the step relation and rewrite
equivalence target $\Type$ rather than $\Prop$:
\[
\Step : \Path(a, b) \to \Path(a, b) \to \Type,
\qquad
\RwEq : \Path(a, b) \to \Path(a, b) \to \Type.
\]
If $\RwEq$ lived in $\Prop$, all rewrite derivations connecting two
paths would be identified by proof irrelevance, collapsing the higher
structure.  When a $\Prop$-valued wrapper is needed, we use
$\mathsf{RwEqProp}(p, q) \defeq \mathsf{Nonempty}(\RwEq(p, q))$.

\emph{Inductive 2-cells and 3-cells.}
The $\mathsf{Derivation}_2$ type is defined as an inductive type in
$\Type\,(u+1)$, with constructors for identity, step, inverse, and
vertical composition.  The $\mathsf{Derivation}_3$ type similarly uses
\texttt{MetaStep3} constructors that encode the named coherence laws
(pentagon, triangle, interchange, inverse/double-inverse coherence,
contravariance).  In the verification suite, every one of the fourteen
named coherence proofs fails if its witness is replaced by
$\mathsf{Derivation}_3.\mathsf{refl}$---confirming that the 3-cell layer
is genuinely non-trivial.

In Lean~4, the core definitions look as follows.  The path type:
\begin{quote}
\small\ttfamily
structure Path (a b : A) where\\
\quad steps : List (Step A)\\
\quad proof : a = b
\end{quote}
The primitive rewrite steps (77 constructors):
\begin{quote}
\small\ttfamily
inductive Step : Path a b $\to$ Path a b $\to$ Type where\\
\quad | symm\_refl : Step (symm (refl a)) (refl a)\\
\quad | symm\_symm : Step (symm (symm p)) p\\
\quad | trans\_refl\_left : Step (trans (refl a) p) p\\
\quad | ...
\end{quote}
The 2-cell type:
\begin{quote}
\small\ttfamily
inductive Derivation2 : Path a b $\to$ Path a b $\to$ Type where\\
\quad | refl (p : Path a b) : Derivation2 p p\\
\quad | step : Step p q $\to$ Derivation2 p q\\
\quad | inv : Derivation2 p q $\to$ Derivation2 q p\\
\quad | vcomp : Derivation2 p q $\to$ Derivation2 q r $\to$ Derivation2 p r
\end{quote}

\emph{Whiskering and horizontal composition.}
Whiskering operations are defined by recursion on the 2-cell constructors,
using the congruence rules for $\trans$.  Horizontal composition is then
defined as in \Cref{def:hcomp}.

\emph{Verification methodology.}
The formalisation uses no axioms beyond the standard Lean~4 kernel
(including propositional extensionality and quotient types, both of
which are axioms in Lean~4 but standard in classical mathematics).
No \texttt{sorry}, \texttt{admit}, or custom \texttt{axiom} declarations
are used.  The build produces over 10{,}000 lemmas and theorems across
approximately 50{,}000 lines of Lean~4 code.

\subsection{Scale and verification}

The formalisation is publicly available at:
\begin{center}
\url{https://github.com/Arthur742Ramos/ComputationalPathsLean}
\end{center}

\noindent
It covers: the groupoid structure; the 77-rule rewrite system (Category~A
fully formalised); confluence for the completed groupoid fragment; the
pentagon and triangle coherences; the Eckmann--Hilton argument; the
$\omega$-groupoid structure with contractibility above dimension~3;

A companion formalisation~\cite{RamosDeOliveiraDeQueirozDeVeras2025lean4}

of lambda calculi with products and sums, establishing the metatheoretic
foundations on which the path rewrite system rests.

\section{Perspectives and Open Problems}\label{sec:perspectives}
% =========================================================================

\begin{problem}[Extension to dependent types and universes]
\label{prob:dependent}
The framework operates on simple types.  Extending to full dependent type
theory requires defining \emph{dependent computational paths}
$\mathsf{DPath}_B(p)(b, b')$, with step preservation, groupoid law
extension, and J-elimination.  Universes would require paths between
types $\Path(A, B)$.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Univalence]\label{prob:univalence}
Whether computational paths can model the univalence axiom remains open.
A partial univalence principle (type equivalences give rise to paths,
without the converse) has been established, but full univalence would
require a significant extension.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Directed computational paths]\label{prob:directed}
A directed variant, where paths model irreversible computation steps,
would connect to directed type theory~\cite{RiehlShulman17} and
$(\infty, 1)$-categories.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Computational complexity]\label{prob:complexity}
While the 77-rule system terminates and is confluent, the precise
computational complexity of normalisation has not been characterised.
The normalisation function $\mathsf{normalize}(p) = \mathsf{sc}(\mathsf{toEq}(p))$
computes in time $O(|p|)$ via semantic projection, but normalisation
by repeated rule application may require $O(|p|^2)$ steps due to
associativity rearrangements.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Applications to program verification]\label{prob:verification}
Computational paths provide explicit witnesses for program equivalence.
Developing this into a practical tool for verified compilation---where
compiler transformations are certified by paths between source and
target programs---is a natural application.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Higher inductive types]\label{prob:hits}
A systematic treatment of higher inductive types~\cite{hottbook} within the
computational-paths framework---with explicit rewrite rules for path
constructors---would extend the topological applications significantly.
The circle $S^1$ used in \Cref{thm:pi1-computations} is a simple instance;
the general theory would require new rule categories for path constructors
and their elimination principles.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Grothendieck's homotopy hypothesis]\label{prob:grothendieck}
Grothendieck's homotopy hypothesis~\cite{Grothendieck83} posits that
$\omega$-groupoids model homotopy types.  Our weak $\omega$-groupoid
construction provides a concrete syntactic candidate.  Establishing a
formal comparison between the fundamental $\omega$-groupoid
$\Pi_\omega(A)$ (defined as the colimit of the computational path tower)
and the classifying space $B(\Pi_\omega(A))$ would connect the theory
directly to classical homotopy theory.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Double groupoid and cubical structure]\label{prob:double}
The 2-cells of computational paths admit two natural source-target
structures (vertical and horizontal), giving rise to a \emph{double
groupoid} in the sense of Brown--Spencer~\cite{BrownSpencer76}.
Relating this double groupoid to the cubical structure of cubical type
theory~\cite{CCHM18,BrownHiggins81} is an open question.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Automation and tooling]\label{prob:automation}
The decidability of path equivalence (\Cref{cor:decidable}) invites
implementation as a tactic in proof assistants.  A tactic that
automatically normalises path expressions and checks equivalence would
significantly streamline proofs involving equality reasoning.  The
challenge is efficiency: while the semantic projection is $O(|p|)$,
integrating it with the proof assistant's elaboration pipeline
requires careful engineering.
\end{problem}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =========================================================================

The theory of computational paths provides a proof-relevant, constructive
account of equality in which the \emph{reasons} for equality are
first-class mathematical objects.  Starting from the labelled natural
deduction framework of de~Queiroz and
Gabbay~\cite{GabbaydeQueiroz92:c-h:full,Gabbay96:LDS}, we have developed
a complete rewrite calculus (77 rules, confluent and terminating), shown
that it gives rise to a weak $\omega$-groupoid structure
(with stabilisation above dimension~3), derived the J-eliminator from
contractibility of the based path space, established the failure of UIP,
and computed fundamental groups of combinatorial spaces.

The distinctive feature of this approach is its concreteness: every
coherence witness is a specific derivation from the rewrite rules, not
an abstract existence claim.  The guiding principle---that all higher
structure is extracted from rewriting---connects proof theory, term
rewriting, higher category theory, and algebraic topology in a unified
framework.  The Lean~4 formalisation (over 10{,}000 theorems, no axioms)
provides machine-checked verification of the central results.

By making the computational content of equality explicit, the theory
offers a foundation for studying the higher-dimensional structure of
type-theoretic equality that is complementary to---and interacts
fruitfully with---the axiomatic approach of homotopy type theory.
